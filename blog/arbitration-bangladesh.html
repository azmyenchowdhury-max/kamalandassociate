Domestic & International Arbitration in Bangladesh: Process, Framework & Legal Strategy

Adv. Mohammad Mostofa Kamal
Senior Partner & Managing Advocate, Kamal & Associates
February 4, 2026 11 min read

Primary Keyword: Arbitration Bangladesh

Secondary Keywords: international arbitration Bangladesh • Arbitration Act 2001 Bangladesh • commercial arbitration Bangladesh • enforcement of arbitral award Bangladesh • dispute resolution Bangladesh • arbitration clause Bangladesh • ICC arbitration Bangladesh • investment arbitration Bangladesh

Key Legal Points
  • Bangladesh's Arbitration Act, 2001 is modelled on the UNCITRAL Model Law on International Commercial Arbitration and governs both domestic and international arbitration proceedings seated in Bangladesh.
  • Bangladesh acceded to the New York Convention on the Recognition and Enforcement of Foreign Arbitral Awards in 1992 — making foreign arbitral awards from Convention signatory states generally enforceable in Bangladesh courts.
  • An arbitration agreement must be in writing under the Arbitration Act, 2001 — and can be contained in a contract clause, a separate arbitration agreement, or an exchange of letters, telegrams, or electronic communications.
  • The High Court Division of the Supreme Court of Bangladesh has supervisory jurisdiction over arbitral proceedings, including the power to grant interim measures, appoint arbitrators, and hear challenges to arbitral awards on limited statutory grounds.
  • Bangladesh has signed bilateral investment treaties (BITs) with more than thirty countries, many of which contain investor-state dispute settlement (ISDS) provisions allowing foreign investors to refer disputes directly to international arbitration without exhausting local courts.

Introduction

As Bangladesh emerges as one of South Asia's fastest-growing economies, commercial disputes — whether between domestic parties or involving foreign investors and multinational corporations — are an inevitable feature of a dynamic business environment. Courts remain the default forum for dispute resolution, but litigation in Bangladesh can be time-consuming, technically complex, and — in cross-border disputes — jurisdictionally complicated.

Arbitration offers a compelling alternative. It is private, confidential, and party-driven — allowing businesses to choose their arbitrators, the governing law, the language of proceedings, and the seat of arbitration. For international commercial transactions in particular, arbitration provides an enforceable mechanism for resolving disputes that transcends the limitations of any single national court system.

Bangladesh has built a solid legislative foundation for arbitration over the past two decades. The Arbitration Act, 2001, New York Convention membership, and a growing number of international investment treaty commitments together create a framework that — when properly utilised — gives parties genuine confidence that their disputes will be resolved efficiently and that any award obtained can be enforced globally. This article explains how arbitration works in Bangladesh, the key legal considerations, and how Kamal & Associates helps clients navigate this increasingly important area of dispute resolution.

Legal Framework in Bangladesh

1. The Arbitration Act, 2001

The Arbitration Act, 2001 is the primary legislation governing arbitration in Bangladesh. It applies to all arbitrations — domestic and international — where the seat of arbitration is in Bangladesh. The Act:

  • Gives legal effect to written arbitration agreements, including clauses in commercial contracts;
  • Provides a framework for the constitution of arbitral tribunals, including default rules for appointing arbitrators where parties fail to agree;
  • Confers authority on the High Court Division to grant interim relief, order preservation of assets, and appoint arbitrators where the agreed procedure breaks down;
  • Sets out the conduct of arbitral proceedings, including rules on pleadings, evidence, hearings, and the deliberation and issuance of awards;
  • Provides grounds for challenging and setting aside arbitral awards — limited to serious procedural irregularities, excess of jurisdiction, or public policy violations — reflecting the international consensus in favour of minimal court intervention in arbitration;
  • Establishes the mechanism for recognition and enforcement of domestic awards.

2. The New York Convention

Bangladesh's accession to the New York Convention on the Recognition and Enforcement of Foreign Arbitral Awards in 1992 is one of the most significant features of its arbitration landscape for international parties. Under the Convention — to which over 170 countries are signatories — an arbitral award made in one contracting state is enforceable in any other contracting state, subject only to limited grounds for refusal (such as incapacity, lack of a valid arbitration agreement, violation of natural justice, or public policy). This means that an award obtained in a Bangladesh-seated arbitration can in principle be enforced in the United States, the United Kingdom, Singapore, the UAE, and other major commercial jurisdictions — and vice versa.

3. Bilateral Investment Treaties and Investor-State Arbitration

Bangladesh has concluded bilateral investment treaties (BITs) with over thirty countries, including the United States, the United Kingdom, Germany, France, China, and India. Most of these BITs contain investor-state dispute settlement (ISDS) clauses under which foreign investors may refer qualifying disputes with the Bangladesh government directly to international arbitration — typically under ICSID (International Centre for Settlement of Investment Disputes), UNCITRAL, or ICC rules — without being required to exhaust local remedies. This is a critical protection for foreign investors undertaking significant capital commitments in Bangladesh.

4. Institutional and Ad Hoc Arbitration

Arbitration in Bangladesh may be institutional — conducted under the rules of an established arbitral institution such as the International Chamber of Commerce (ICC), the Singapore International Arbitration Centre (SIAC), the London Court of International Arbitration (LCIA), or the Bangladesh International Arbitration Centre (BIAC) — or ad hoc, conducted under rules agreed by the parties (often UNCITRAL Rules) without the oversight of an institution. BIAC, established in 2011 under The Bangladesh International Arbitration Centre Act, 2011, provides an institutional framework specifically designed for regional and Bangladesh-related commercial disputes and is increasingly used by domestic and regional parties.

Domestic Arbitration vs. International Arbitration

The Arbitration Act, 2001 draws a distinction between domestic arbitration (where all parties are Bangladesh nationals or entities, or where the seat and governing law are both in Bangladesh) and international commercial arbitration (where at least one party is a foreign national, foreign company, or foreign state, or where the parties have agreed that the subject matter relates to more than one country).

The distinction matters for several reasons. International commercial arbitrations are subject to certain additional provisions of the Act, and the enforcement regime for awards made in international proceedings may differ procedurally. In practice, most large commercial disputes involving foreign parties will be structured as international commercial arbitrations — often with a neutral foreign seat such as Singapore, London, or Paris — while purely domestic commercial disputes between Bangladesh parties will typically use BIAC or conduct ad hoc arbitration seated in Dhaka.

Common Legal Issues

1. Poorly Drafted Arbitration Clauses

The single most common problem in commercial arbitration is a defective arbitration clause. Vague, ambiguous, or pathological clauses — such as those that specify an institution that no longer exists, fail to identify the seat of arbitration, or contain contradictory dispute resolution mechanisms — can result in satellite litigation over the validity and scope of the arbitration agreement before any substantive proceedings can begin. This litigation is expensive, time-consuming, and entirely avoidable with careful drafting at the contract stage.

2. Jurisdiction Disputes

Respondents in arbitration — particularly those who have assessed that the merits of the claim are difficult to defend — frequently challenge the arbitral tribunal's jurisdiction. Common grounds include arguments that the arbitration agreement is invalid, that the dispute falls outside the scope of the arbitration clause, or that the correct seat is in a different jurisdiction. Under the Arbitration Act, 2001, the tribunal has the power to rule on its own jurisdiction (the kompetenz-kompetenz principle), but jurisdictional challenges can significantly delay and complicate proceedings.

3. Enforcement of Foreign Arbitral Awards

While Bangladesh's New York Convention membership creates a strong presumption in favour of enforcing foreign awards, enforcement applications before the Bangladesh courts are not always straightforward. Respondents may raise public policy objections or procedural grounds to resist enforcement. The practical efficiency of enforcement proceedings — and the likelihood of success in resisting spurious objections — depends heavily on the expertise of local counsel managing the enforcement application.

4. Challenging Domestic Arbitral Awards

Under the Arbitration Act, 2001, a party may apply to the High Court Division to set aside a domestic arbitral award on limited grounds: incapacity of a party, invalidity of the arbitration agreement, breach of natural justice, excess of jurisdiction, or violation of public policy. These grounds are intentionally narrow — the Act reflects the international principle that court interference with arbitral awards should be the exception rather than the rule. Courts that allow set-aside applications on substantive merits grounds — second-guessing the tribunal's factual or legal conclusions — are not consistent with the Act's intent.

5. Interim Measures and Emergency Relief

In urgent cases — where assets may be dissipated, evidence destroyed, or a party's interests irreparably damaged before an arbitral tribunal is constituted — parties may apply to the High Court Division under the Arbitration Act, 2001 for interim relief to preserve the status quo. The availability and effectiveness of interim relief is a critical practical consideration in any commercial arbitration strategy, and requires immediate expert legal input.

6. Multi-party and Multi-contract Disputes

Modern commercial transactions frequently involve multiple parties across multiple contracts — for example, in construction projects involving an employer, contractor, sub-contractors, and funders, each under different contractual arrangements with potentially different dispute resolution clauses. Managing related disputes across multiple arbitral proceedings — including applications to consolidate, joinder of additional parties, or parallel court proceedings — requires sophisticated case management strategy.

Arbitration requires specialist expertise that is distinct from general litigation practice. Kamal & Associates provides comprehensive arbitration services across the full lifecycle of a dispute — from contract drafting through to enforcement or challenge of the final award:

  • Pre-arbitration strategy: Advising on whether to initiate arbitration or seek negotiated resolution first, assessing whether contractual conditions precedent (such as mandatory mediation or notice periods) have been satisfied, and mapping out the procedural roadmap for the most efficient possible proceeding.
  • Arbitrator selection and challenge: Advising on the selection of arbitrators with the appropriate subject matter expertise, nationality, and availability — and managing any conflicts of interest or challenges to appointed arbitrators under the relevant institutional rules or the Arbitration Act, 2001.
  • Conducting arbitral proceedings: Preparing and submitting statements of claim and defense, conducting document production, preparing and examining witnesses, drafting expert evidence instructions and comments, making oral and written submissions on both jurisdiction and merits, and managing all procedural steps through to final award.
  • Interim relief applications: Filing and arguing urgent applications to the High Court Division for interim measures to preserve assets, maintain the status quo, or secure evidence pending constitution of the tribunal or during proceedings.
  • Enforcement of awards: Preparing and filing enforcement applications under the New York Convention or the Arbitration Act, 2001, responding to set-aside applications, and advising on practical enforcement strategy across Bangladesh and internationally.
  • Investment treaty claims: Advising foreign investors on whether qualifying treatment by the Bangladesh government triggers ISDS protection under an applicable BIT, and managing investor-state arbitration proceedings before ICSID, ICC, or under UNCITRAL Rules.

Practical Tips for Clients

  • Review arbitration clauses in every commercial contract before signing: An arbitration clause agreed at the contract stage determines the entire dispute resolution landscape for the life of the agreement. Invest in specialist review before signing, not after a dispute emerges.
  • Choose your seat carefully: The seat of arbitration determines the law governing the arbitral process (the lex arbitri), the courts with supervisory jurisdiction, and — often — how easily an award can be enforced. Singapore, London, Paris, and Hong Kong are favoured neutral seats for international commercial transactions involving South Asian parties. Dhaka (BIAC) is an appropriate seat for purely domestic or regional disputes.
  • Act as soon as a dispute becomes apparent: Limitation periods apply in arbitration as in litigation. In Bangladesh, the Limitation Act, 1908 applies to arbitration claims. Delay in initiating proceedings can bar potentially meritorious claims entirely.
  • Preserve all documentary evidence from the outset: Documentary evidence — contracts, correspondence, emails, payment records, project records — is the backbone of most commercial arbitrations. Implement a document preservation protocol as soon as a dispute is anticipated and before any routine document deletion cycles apply.
  • Understand the distinction between arbitration and mediation: Arbitration produces a binding, enforceable award. Mediation produces a negotiated settlement only if both parties agree. Many commercial contracts specify a staged ADR process — typically mediation first, arbitration if mediation fails. Understanding which process you are in, and what your obligations are at each stage, is critical to protecting your legal position.
  • Engage specialist arbitration counsel, not general litigators: Arbitration — particularly international commercial or investment treaty arbitration — is a highly specialised field. General litigation experience is a starting point but is insufficient. The procedural, evidentiary, and strategic requirements of arbitration are distinct from court proceedings, and the consequences of procedural missteps in arbitration can be severe and unreviewable.
Practical Insight

The most significant arbitration advantage that sophisticated commercial parties frequently underutilise is the freedom to choose their arbitrators. Unlike litigation — where the judge is assigned without party input — arbitration allows parties to select arbitrators with specific industry expertise, technical knowledge, and familiarity with the applicable law. The quality of an arbitral tribunal is the single most important variable in the quality and efficiency of the outcome. Investing time and legal advice in arbitrator selection is among the highest-value decisions in any commercial arbitration. Both domestic and international parties have access to a dispute resolution mechanism that is private, enforceable across jurisdictions, and — when properly managed — significantly faster and more flexible than court litigation.

FAQs

1. Is an arbitration agreement compulsorily enforceable in Bangladesh?
Yes. Under Section 7 of the Arbitration Act, 2001, a valid written arbitration agreement is binding and enforceable. If one party commences court proceedings in breach of an arbitration agreement, the other party may apply to the court to stay the court proceedings and refer the dispute to arbitration. The court must grant the stay unless the arbitration agreement is null and void, inoperative, or incapable of being performed. This reflects the international principle of giving effect to the parties' contractual choice of arbitration as the exclusive dispute resolution mechanism.

2. Can a foreign arbitral award be enforced in Bangladesh?
Yes. Bangladesh's accession to the New York Convention in 1992 means that arbitral awards made in any other Convention signatory state can be enforced in Bangladesh by filing an enforcement application in the High Court Division of the Supreme Court. The grounds for refusing enforcement are limited — they mirror the New York Convention Article V grounds — and include incapacity of a party, absence of a valid arbitration agreement, violation of natural justice, excess of the tribunal's jurisdiction, procedural impropriety in constituting the tribunal, or conflict with Bangladesh public policy. These grounds are narrow and courts are expected to interpret them restrictively, consistent with Bangladesh's international obligations.

3. What is the Bangladesh International Arbitration Centre (BIAC) and when should I use it?
BIAC is Bangladesh's primary domestic arbitral institution, established under The Bangladesh International Arbitration Centre Act, 2011. It administers arbitral proceedings under its own rules — covering case management, arbitrator appointment, fees, and administration — and provides hearing facilities in Dhaka. BIAC is an appropriate choice for domestic commercial disputes between Bangladesh parties, and for regional cross-border transactions involving parties from South and Southeast Asia who prefer a local institutional framework. For high-value international disputes involving parties from major commercial jurisdictions, parties often prefer established international institutions such as ICC, SIAC, or LCIA — though all of these can conduct proceedings seated in Dhaka if the parties so choose.

4. What are the grounds for challenging an arbitral award in Bangladesh?
Under Section 42 of the Arbitration Act, 2001, a party may apply to the High Court Division to set aside a domestic arbitral award only on the grounds that: (i) a party was under some incapacity; (ii) the arbitration agreement is invalid under the law applicable to it; (iii) the applicant was not given proper notice of the arbitral proceedings or was otherwise unable to present their case; (iv) the award deals with a dispute not falling within the scope of the arbitration agreement; (v) the composition of the tribunal or the conduct of proceedings was contrary to the parties' agreement or the Act; or (vi) the subject matter of the dispute is not capable of settlement by arbitration under Bangladesh law, or the award is in conflict with the public policy of Bangladesh. Importantly, dissatisfaction with the tribunal's substantive legal or factual conclusions is not a valid ground for setting aside an award — the court does not re-hear the merits.

5. How long does an arbitration typically take in Bangladesh?
The duration of arbitral proceedings varies significantly depending on the complexity of the dispute, the number of parties and issues, the efficiency of the arbitral tribunal, and how the parties conduct the proceedings. A straightforward domestic commercial arbitration managed efficiently by an experienced tribunal can conclude within six to twelve months. Complex multi-party, multi-contract, or technically intensive disputes — particularly in construction, energy, or financial services — can take two to four years, though this compares very favourably with the timeline for equivalent court proceedings in Bangladesh. Careful procedural management by specialist counsel — including robust timetabling, focused pleadings, disciplined document production, and tight control of witness and expert evidence — has a significant impact on the speed and cost of arbitral proceedings.
