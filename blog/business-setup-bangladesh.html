Introduction

Business Setup in Bangladesh has become a major priority for local entrepreneurs, expatriate founders, and foreign investors looking at South Asian growth opportunities. Bangladesh offers a strategic location, growing consumer demand, expanding industrial zones, and improving infrastructure. However, business opportunity does not eliminate legal risk.

A large number of businesses face avoidable delays because they focus on registration speed but ignore legal structure, licensing sequence, and post-incorporation compliance. The result can be rejected applications, banking obstacles, tax exposure, shareholder disputes, and regulatory action.

This guide explains how to set up a business in Bangladesh through a legally sound approach. It covers the relevant laws, institutions, practical pitfalls, and strategic legal protections that support long-term growth.

Legal Framework in Bangladesh

1) Constitutional and Institutional Context

The Constitution of Bangladesh creates the broader legal environment for lawful commercial activity and judicial remedies. Business disputes and regulatory decisions can ultimately be reviewed by the High Court Division and Appellate Division of the Supreme Court where legal rights are affected.

2) Companies Act, 1994 and RJSC

The Companies Act, 1994 is the primary law for incorporation, shareholding structure, director responsibilities, governance, and statutory filings. The Registrar of Joint Stock Companies and Firms (RJSC) handles name clearance, incorporation filings, and company record administration.

3) Contract Act, 1872 and Commercial Documentation

From the first day of operation, contracts govern supplier relationships, leases, services, employment, and financing. The Contract Act, 1872 shapes enforceability and risk allocation. Weak contracts are one of the most common causes of business litigation.

4) Labour Act, 2006 and Workforce Compliance

When a company hires employees, compliance under the Labour Act, 2006 becomes essential. Appointment terms, wages, leave, workplace obligations, and separation process should be legally documented from the outset.

5) Tax, VAT, and Banking Compliance

Business setup requires more than RJSC registration. Most businesses need e-TIN, VAT/BIN, and trade license before full operation. For foreign investment, remittance channels and repatriation matters must comply with Bangladesh Bank rules and applicable foreign exchange regulations.

6) BIDA and Sectoral Permissions

The Bangladesh Investment Development Authority (BIDA) is relevant for certain foreign investment processes, branch/liaison permissions, and investor facilitation. Sector-specific approvals may also be required for telecom, healthcare, manufacturing, environment, fire safety, or municipal compliance.

Common Legal Issues

1) Wrong Entity Selection

Choosing an unsuitable legal structure can create tax inefficiency, investor-entry problems, and governance conflicts. Entity type should match ownership, capital strategy, and operational model.

2) Name Clearance and Object Clause Problems

RJSC name rejection and narrow business objects in the MoA can delay registration and limit future expansion. Correct drafting at setup stage prevents repeated filings later.

3) Incomplete Shareholder Protection

Founders often skip shareholders' agreements or rely on generic templates. This leads to disputes over voting, exit rights, dilution, board control, and dividend policy.

4) Foreign Investment Documentation Errors

Improper remittance records, missing approvals, or unclear ownership documentation can affect compliance and future repatriation rights.

5) Licensing Sequence Mistakes

Many businesses incorporate first but delay essential operating licenses. This creates exposure to enforcement action and operational disruption.

6) Post-Incorporation Non-Compliance

Failure to maintain annual returns, board resolutions, labor records, and tax filings can result in penalties and legal vulnerability.

Practical Tips for Clients

  • Start with legal planning: Finalize ownership split, board control, and investor strategy before filing.
  • Prepare multiple name options: Keep backup names ready to avoid registration delay.
  • Future-proof business objects: Draft MoA clauses to cover realistic medium-term expansion.
  • Set compliance calendar early: Track filing deadlines, tax obligations, and governance formalities.
  • Document foreign funds correctly: Maintain complete remittance trail and board approvals.
  • Use written contracts for key operations: Lease, supply, employment, and service terms should be legally reviewed.

Conclusion

Business Setup in Bangladesh should be approached as a legal strategy, not only an administrative process. Proper structure, accurate documentation, and disciplined compliance can protect promoters, attract investors, and prevent costly disputes.

FAQs

1. How long does business registration usually take in Bangladesh?
Timelines vary by case and document quality, but with proper preparation many incorporations move efficiently through RJSC processing.

2. Can a foreign investor own 100% of a company?
In many sectors, yes. However, compliance with applicable foreign investment and banking rules is essential.

3. Is RJSC registration enough to start operations?
Usually no. Most businesses also require trade license, tax registration, VAT/BIN, and sector-specific permissions.

4. Why is a shareholders' agreement important?
It helps prevent disputes by clearly defining voting rights, transfer restrictions, management control, and exit mechanisms.

5. What is the biggest legal mistake in early-stage setup?
Skipping legal planning and relying on generic documents is one of the most common and costly mistakes.
