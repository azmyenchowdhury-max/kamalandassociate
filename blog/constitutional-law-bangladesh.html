Constitutional Law in Bangladesh: Rights, Remedies & Writ Jurisdiction

Adv. Mustafa Kamal Chowdhury
Senior Advocate, High Court Division
February 1, 2026 10 min read

Primary Keyword: Constitutional Law in Bangladesh

Secondary Keywords: fundamental rights Bangladesh • writ petition Bangladesh • judicial review Bangladesh • Article 102 Bangladesh Constitution • High Court Division writ jurisdiction • right to equality Bangladesh • constitutional remedies Bangladesh

Key Legal Points
  • The Constitution of Bangladesh (1972) is the supreme law — any law inconsistent with it is void to the extent of the inconsistency.
  • Part III of the Constitution (Articles 26–47A) guarantees fundamental rights enforceable directly through the courts.
  • Article 102 empowers the High Court Division to issue writs of certiorari, mandamus, prohibition, habeas corpus, and quo warranto.
  • The Supreme Court of Bangladesh — comprising the High Court Division and the Appellate Division — is the ultimate guardian of the Constitution.
  • Constitutional amendments require a two-thirds majority in Parliament, and core provisions cannot be altered in ways that destroy the basic structure of the Constitution.

Introduction

Constitutional law in Bangladesh forms the bedrock of the entire legal system. It defines the relationship between the state and the individual, distributes power among the three branches of government — the legislature, the executive, and the judiciary — and guarantees fundamental rights that no ordinary law can take away.

For businesses, foreign investors, property buyers, and individuals, understanding constitutional law is not merely academic. It is a practical necessity. If a government authority acts unlawfully, if a regulation infringes your rights, or if public officials abuse their power, constitutional law provides the tools to fight back. The High Court Division of the Supreme Court of Bangladesh can be petitioned to issue writs — powerful court orders that restrain unlawful state action and enforce fundamental rights.

This guide explains the structure of Bangladesh's constitutional framework, the fundamental rights available to citizens and non-citizens, the writ jurisdiction of the High Court Division, and how Kamal & Associates supports clients in constitutional matters — from advising on rights violations to filing writ petitions before the Supreme Court.

Legal Framework in Bangladesh

1. The Constitution of Bangladesh, 1972

The Constitution of Bangladesh came into force on 16 December 1972, following the independence of Bangladesh in 1971. It is the supreme law of the country. Article 7 declares that all powers of the Republic belong to the people, and the Constitution is the solemn expression of the will of the people. Any law inconsistent with the Constitution is void to the extent of the inconsistency.

The Constitution establishes a parliamentary form of government, with the President as the constitutional head and the Prime Minister as the chief executive. It creates an independent judiciary, a unicameral Parliament (Jatiya Sangsad), and a framework for separation of powers and checks and balances.

2. Fundamental Rights — Part III of the Constitution

Articles 26 to 47A of the Constitution guarantee a wide range of fundamental rights. These include:

  • Right to equality before law (Article 27): All citizens are equal before the law and entitled to equal protection of the law.
  • Prohibition of discrimination (Article 28): The State shall not discriminate against any citizen on grounds of religion, race, caste, sex, or place of birth.
  • Equality of opportunity in public employment (Article 29): Equal opportunity for all citizens in government employment, subject to permissible reservations.
  • Protection of right to life and personal liberty (Article 32): No person shall be deprived of life or personal liberty save in accordance with law.
  • Protection against arbitrary arrest and detention (Article 33): The right to be informed of grounds of arrest and to consult and be defended by a legal practitioner.
  • Freedom of movement (Article 36): Every citizen has the right to move freely throughout Bangladesh and to reside and settle in any place therein.
  • Freedom of thought, conscience, and speech (Article 39): Freedom of thought and conscience, and freedom of speech and expression, subject to reasonable restrictions.
  • Freedom of association (Article 38): Every citizen has the right to form associations or unions.
  • Freedom of religion (Article 41): Every citizen has the right to profess, practise, or propagate their religion.
  • Right to property (Article 42): Every citizen has the right to acquire, hold, transfer or otherwise dispose of property, subject to reasonable restrictions imposed by law.

These rights are not absolute. Many are subject to reasonable restrictions by law in the interests of national security, public order, public morality, or the rights of others. However, restrictions must be proportionate and lawful — they cannot be used to destroy the substance of the right itself.

3. Writ Jurisdiction — Article 102

Article 102 of the Constitution grants the High Court Division the power to issue directions, declarations, or orders in the nature of habeas corpus, mandamus, certiorari, prohibition, and quo warranto. This writ jurisdiction is one of the most powerful constitutional tools available to citizens and affected persons.

  • Habeas corpus: Challenges unlawful detention or arrest. The court can order the release of a person being held illegally.
  • Mandamus: Compels a public authority to perform a public duty it has unlawfully refused or neglected to perform.
  • Certiorari: Quashes unlawful decisions made by statutory bodies, government agencies, or courts acting beyond their jurisdiction.
  • Prohibition: Restrains a lower court or tribunal from acting beyond its jurisdiction, before the ultra vires action is completed.
  • Quo warranto: Challenges the lawfulness of a person's claim to hold a public office or position.

The Appellate Division of the Supreme Court hears appeals from the High Court Division and has original jurisdiction in constitutional matters reserved by the Constitution.

4. Judicial Independence and the Basic Structure Doctrine

Bangladesh courts have developed a "basic structure" doctrine, drawing on comparative constitutional jurisprudence. Core constitutional features — such as the supremacy of the Constitution, judicial independence, the democratic framework, and fundamental rights — cannot be removed even through formal constitutional amendment. This doctrine protects the constitutional order from being dismantled through parliamentary majority alone.

5. National Human Rights Commission

The National Human Rights Commission Act, 2009 established the National Human Rights Commission (NHRC), which investigates complaints concerning violations of human rights. While the NHRC cannot enforce court orders, its findings carry persuasive weight and can support subsequent legal proceedings, including writ petitions before the High Court Division.

Common Legal Issues

1. Unlawful Detention and Arrest

Citizens are sometimes held in custody beyond the 24-hour limit without being produced before a magistrate, or detained under special powers without proper cause. Habeas corpus petitions under Article 102 are the direct remedy, and courts can order immediate production and release where detention is found illegal.

2. Arbitrary Administrative Decisions

Government departments, tax authorities, licensing bodies, and regulatory agencies sometimes take decisions that are procedurally unfair, based on irrelevant considerations, or plainly ultra vires their statutory powers. A writ of certiorari can quash such decisions and force a lawful reconsideration.

3. Discrimination in Public Employment and Services

Individuals and businesses sometimes face discriminatory treatment in government service delivery or public employment decisions made on unlawful grounds. Articles 27 and 28 of the Constitution provide the basis to challenge such discrimination before the High Court Division.

4. Infringement of Property Rights

Compulsory acquisition of land or property by the state without fair compensation, or administrative interference with property rights without lawful authority, can be challenged constitutionally under Article 42, which protects the right to property subject to specific legal conditions.

5. Press Freedom and Expression Rights

Journalists, media organisations, and individuals sometimes face regulatory action or legal proceedings that restrict freedom of speech and expression guaranteed under Article 39. Where such restrictions are disproportionate or unlawful, constitutional remedies are available.

6. Challenges to Subordinate Legislation

Statutory rules, regulations, and notifications issued by government ministries or agencies may be challenged if they exceed the parent Act's authority or are inconsistent with fundamental rights. The High Court Division can declare such subordinate legislation invalid.

Practical Tips for Clients

  • Act quickly: Writ petitions must generally be filed within a reasonable time of the alleged wrongful act. Unnecessary delay can lead to the court refusing relief on grounds of laches.
  • Document everything: Keep copies of all government orders, notices, correspondence, and decisions that you intend to challenge. Courts require documentary evidence to assess constitutional claims.
  • Exhaust statutory remedies first (where applicable): In some situations, courts may require that available statutory appeals or review mechanisms be exhausted before a writ is entertained.
  • Understand the distinction between constitutional and ordinary remedies: Not every grievance against the government is a constitutional violation. Seek legal advice to determine whether your situation warrants a writ petition or an alternative remedy.
  • Engage qualified constitutional counsel: Constitutional litigation requires familiarity with case law, court procedure, and the specific standing rules of the High Court Division. Specialist representation significantly improves outcomes.
  • Preserve limitation periods: Even outside formal writ requirements, important deadlines may apply to related statutory appeals. Missing them can close off alternative remedies entirely.
Practical Insight

In Bangladesh, constitutional litigation is often the fastest route to relief when ordinary legal remedies are unavailable or inadequate. For instance, a writ of habeas corpus can be heard on the same day it is filed in urgent cases, making it one of the most immediate legal tools available. Similarly, where a government agency refuses to grant a licence or permit that it is legally obliged to issue, a writ of mandamus can compel compliance without waiting years for a civil suit to be determined.

Conclusion

Constitutional law in Bangladesh is far more than a theoretical subject. It is a living, enforceable body of law that protects citizens, businesses, and institutions from unlawful state action. The Constitution guarantees fundamental rights, empowers courts to review government decisions, and provides direct remedies — including powerful writ orders — for those whose rights are violated.

FAQs

1. Who can file a writ petition under Article 102 of the Bangladesh Constitution?
Any person whose fundamental rights have been infringed or who is aggrieved by unlawful state action can file a writ petition before the High Court Division. In public interest cases, a petition may be filed even by a person not directly affected, provided there is a genuine public interest at stake.

2. Is there a time limit for filing a writ petition in Bangladesh?
There is no fixed statutory limitation period for writ petitions under Article 102. However, the courts apply the principle of laches — unreasonable delay in seeking relief can result in a petition being dismissed. It is strongly advisable to file as soon as possible after the alleged constitutional violation occurs.

3. Can a foreign national or a company use constitutional remedies in Bangladesh?
Most fundamental rights under Part III of the Constitution are available to citizens. However, some rights — such as the right to life and personal liberty under Article 32, and protection from arbitrary arrest under Article 33 — are available to all persons, including foreign nationals. Companies may invoke certain constitutional protections relevant to corporate entities, though citizenship-specific rights do not apply.

4. What is the difference between the High Court Division and the Appellate Division of the Supreme Court?
The High Court Division has original writ jurisdiction under Article 102 and hears cases at first instance. The Appellate Division is the final court of appeal in Bangladesh and hears appeals from decisions of the High Court Division. The Appellate Division's judgments are binding on all other courts in Bangladesh.

5. Can a law passed by Parliament be challenged on constitutional grounds?
Yes. Any law — whether an Act of Parliament, subsidiary legislation, or administrative regulation — that is inconsistent with the Constitution can be challenged before the High Court Division. If the court finds the law unconstitutional, it may declare it void to the extent of the inconsistency.
