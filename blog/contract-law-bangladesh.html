Contract Law in Bangladesh: Formation, Enforcement & Legal Remedies

Adv. Harun Rayhan
Advocate, District & Session Judge Court
February 2, 2026 10 min read

Primary Keyword: Contract Law in Bangladesh

Secondary Keywords: contract enforcement Bangladesh • breach of contract Bangladesh • Contract Act 1872 • void and voidable contracts Bangladesh • commercial contracts Bangladesh • liquidated damages Bangladesh • specific performance Bangladesh

Key Legal Points
  • The Contract Act, 1872 is the primary legislation governing all contracts in Bangladesh — one of the oldest and most comprehensive pieces of commercial legislation in the country.
  • A valid contract requires offer, acceptance, consideration, capacity, free consent, and a lawful object. Absence of any element renders the agreement void or voidable.
  • Contracts obtained through coercion, fraud, undue influence, or misrepresentation are voidable at the option of the aggrieved party.
  • The Specific Relief Act, 1877 empowers courts to order specific performance of a contract where monetary damages are inadequate — particularly relevant in property and commercial transactions.
  • The Limitation Act, 1908 sets a three-year time limit for bringing most breach of contract claims — delay can permanently extinguish the right to sue.

Introduction

Contract law is the foundation of every commercial and legal relationship in Bangladesh. Whether you are a business owner entering a supply agreement, a property developer signing construction contracts, a foreign investor structuring a joint venture, or an individual purchasing goods or services — every transaction rests on a contract. Understanding how contracts work, what makes them enforceable, and what remedies are available when they are breached is not merely an academic exercise. It is a practical necessity.

In Bangladesh, contract law is governed primarily by the Contract Act, 1872 — a statute of enduring importance that defines the rules of offer, acceptance, consideration, capacity, and consent. However, the law does not operate in isolation. Employment contracts are additionally regulated by the Bangladesh Labour Act, 2006. Property transactions engage the Transfer of Property Act, 1882 and the Registration Act, 1908. Financial contracts must comply with Bangladesh Bank regulations. Each context brings its own layer of complexity.

This guide explains the foundations of contract law in Bangladesh, the most common contractual disputes, and how Kamal & Associates assists clients in drafting, reviewing, enforcing, and litigating contractual arrangements.

Legal Framework in Bangladesh

1. The Contract Act, 1872

The Contract Act, 1872 remains the cornerstone of contract law in Bangladesh. Originally enacted during British India and adopted by Bangladesh upon independence, it defines a contract under Section 2(h) as "an agreement enforceable by law." The Act covers the full lifecycle of a contract — from formation and performance through to discharge and remedies for breach.

2. Essential Elements of a Valid Contract

For a contract to be legally binding and enforceable, it must satisfy six essential elements:

  • Offer and Acceptance (Sections 3–9): One party must make a clear, definite offer, and the other must accept it unconditionally and without modification. A counter-offer constitutes a rejection of the original offer and creates a new offer.
  • Consideration (Section 2(d)): Each party must give something of value — an act, forbearance, or promise. Contracts without consideration are unenforceable, subject to limited exceptions under Section 25 such as natural love and affection between close relatives, or past voluntary services.
  • Capacity to Contract (Sections 11–12): Parties must have attained the age of majority (18 years), be of sound mind, and not be disqualified from contracting by any law to which they are subject.
  • Free Consent (Sections 13–22): Consent to a contract must be given freely. The Act identifies five vitiating factors — coercion, undue influence, fraud, misrepresentation, and mistake — any of which can render a contract void or voidable.
  • Lawful Object and Consideration (Section 23): The purpose and consideration of the contract must not be forbidden by law, fraudulent, harmful to others, or opposed to public policy. Contracts with an unlawful object are void.
  • Certainty and Completeness (Section 29): The terms must be sufficiently clear and definite. An agreement that is uncertain or incapable of being made certain is void.

3. Void and Voidable Contracts

Not all agreements are enforceable. The Contract Act draws a critical distinction:

  • Void contracts: Agreements that have no legal effect at all — for example, contracts with minors (void ab initio), agreements in restraint of trade (Section 27), wagering agreements (Section 30), or agreements with an unlawful object (Section 23).
  • Voidable contracts: Agreements that are valid and binding unless and until one party elects to avoid them. A contract is voidable when consent is obtained by coercion, undue influence, fraud, or misrepresentation. The aggrieved party may rescind the contract and seek restoration, or affirm it and claim damages.

4. The Specific Relief Act, 1877

The Specific Relief Act governs the equitable remedies available when a party breaches a contract. Courts may grant specific performance — ordering the defaulting party to fulfil their contractual obligations — where damages are inadequate. This is particularly common in contracts for the sale of immovable property or unique goods, and in commercial arrangements where the subject matter cannot be easily replaced or valued.

5. Relevant Sectoral Legislation

Several types of contracts carry additional regulatory requirements in Bangladesh:

  • Employment contracts: Subject to the Bangladesh Labour Act, 2006 and Bangladesh Labour Rules, 2015, including mandatory written terms for permanent employees.
  • Property contracts: Transfer of Property Act, 1882 and Registration Act, 1908 require written instruments and registration for enforceable property transfers.
  • Financial contracts: Regulated by Bangladesh Bank guidelines, the Bank Company Act, 1991, and related financial sector legislation.
  • Government contracts: Subject to the Public Procurement Act, 2006 and Public Procurement Rules, 2008, with additional transparency and process requirements.

Common Legal Issues

1. Breach of Contract

The most prevalent contractual dispute is breach — where one party fails to perform their obligations. Breach can be total (complete non-performance), partial (incomplete performance), or anticipatory (where a party declares or demonstrates in advance an intention not to perform). Each type carries different consequences and remedies.

2. Disputes Arising from Ambiguous Terms

Vague or loosely drafted contracts routinely produce disputes about the scope of obligations, payment timelines, delivery standards, and liability allocation. Courts are constrained to interpret ambiguous terms rather than rewrite them, and outcomes are unpredictable. Prevention through precise drafting is far more cost-effective than litigation.

3. Non-Payment and Commercial Debt Recovery

Businesses frequently face non-payment for goods delivered or services rendered. In Bangladesh, money recovery suits are filed before civil courts under the Code of Civil Procedure, and can be supplemented by applications for interim injunctions to freeze assets pending judgment. Strong written contracts with clear payment terms significantly improve recovery prospects.

4. Fraudulent Misrepresentation and Rescission

Where one party enters a contract based on false statements or concealed material facts made by the other, the Contract Act provides the defrauded party with the right to rescind the contract and recover any benefit conferred. Establishing fraudulent misrepresentation requires clear evidence, making contemporaneous written records essential.

5. International and Cross-Border Contract Disputes

International commercial contracts introduce additional complexity — choice of governing law, jurisdiction clauses, arbitration agreements, foreign exchange compliance under Bangladesh Bank regulations, and enforcement of foreign judgments or arbitral awards. Foreign investors must ensure their contracts are specifically structured for the Bangladesh legal environment.

6. Construction, Infrastructure, and Services Disputes

Bangladesh's rapidly developing construction and infrastructure sector generates frequent and high-value contract disputes — typically involving payment delays, scope variations, defective workmanship, suspension of works, and termination. These disputes often require both technical and legal expertise to assess and resolve.

Practical Tips for Clients

  • Always put it in writing: Oral contracts are theoretically enforceable in Bangladesh, but proving their existence and terms in court is extremely difficult without corroborating evidence. Always document agreements — even informal ones — in writing.
  • Define terms precisely: Avoid vague expressions such as "reasonable time," "market rate," or "as mutually agreed" without specific definitions. Courts must interpret ambiguity, and outcomes are unpredictable.
  • Include a dispute resolution clause: Specify whether disputes will be resolved by negotiation, mediation, arbitration, or litigation — and in which court, city, or forum. Omitting this clause leads to protracted jurisdictional arguments before the substantive dispute is even heard.
  • Specify governing law in international contracts: Contracts involving foreign parties must state which country's law governs and which courts have jurisdiction. Without this, international disputes can become expensive jurisdictional battles.
  • Keep comprehensive performance records: Preserve all invoices, delivery receipts, correspondence, meeting minutes, and payment records. These documents serve as critical evidence if a dispute arises.
  • Act promptly on breach: The Limitation Act, 1908 imposes a three-year limitation period on most contract claims from the date of breach. Delay can permanently close off the right to sue for damages.
Practical Insight

In Bangladesh's commercial landscape, the most avoidable contract disputes arise not from deliberate bad faith, but from agreements where key terms — payment timelines, delivery standards, termination rights, and liability caps — are left unclear or omitted entirely. A contract reviewed by an experienced commercial lawyer before signing typically costs a small fraction of the cost of litigating a dispute afterwards. Prevention is always the smarter investment.

FAQs

1. Is a verbal contract enforceable in Bangladesh?
Yes. Oral contracts are generally enforceable under the Contract Act, 1872, provided all essential elements are met. However, proving the existence and precise terms of an oral contract in court is extremely difficult without corroborating documentary or witness evidence. Written contracts are strongly recommended for all significant transactions.

2. What is the limitation period for bringing a breach of contract claim in Bangladesh?
Under the Limitation Act, 1908, the general limitation period for breach of contract claims is three years from the date of breach. For contracts involving immovable property or specific statutory instruments, different limitation periods may apply. Seeking legal advice promptly after a breach is discovered is essential to preserve your rights.

3. What remedies are available for breach of contract in Bangladesh?
The primary remedies are: (i) damages — monetary compensation for financial loss caused by the breach; (ii) specific performance — a court order under the Specific Relief Act, 1877 compelling the defaulting party to perform their obligations; (iii) injunction — a court order preventing a threatened or ongoing breach; and (iv) rescission — cancelling the contract and restoring parties to their pre-contract positions.

4. Can a contract entered into with a minor be enforced in Bangladesh?
No. Under Section 11 of the Contract Act, 1872, a person must have attained the age of majority (18 years) to be competent to contract. Agreements with minors are void ab initio — they have no legal effect from the outset and cannot be ratified or enforced against the minor even after they reach adulthood.

5. What is a liquidated damages clause and why is it important?
A liquidated damages clause specifies a pre-agreed sum payable upon breach — for example, a daily penalty for late delivery in a construction contract. Under Section 74 of the Contract Act, courts in Bangladesh will enforce a genuine pre-estimate of loss rather than a penalty. Well-drafted liquidated damages clauses provide certainty and reduce the burden of proving actual loss in litigation, making them a practical tool in commercial contracts.
