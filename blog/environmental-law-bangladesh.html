Environmental Law in Bangladesh: Regulations, Compliance & Legal Remedies

Adv. Mohammad Kabir
Advocate, Supreme Court of Bangladesh
February 6, 2026 11 min read

Primary Keyword: Environmental Law Bangladesh

Secondary Keywords: Environment Conservation Act 1995 • EIA Bangladesh • pollution law Bangladesh • environmental compliance Bangladesh • Environment Court Bangladesh • environmental litigation Bangladesh • industrial pollution Bangladesh • environmental permit Bangladesh

Key Legal Points
  • The Environment Conservation Act, 1995 (amended 2010) and the Environment Conservation Rules, 1997 form the primary legislative framework governing environmental protection and pollution control in Bangladesh — administered by the Department of Environment (DoE) under the Ministry of Environment, Forest and Climate Change.
  • All industrial, commercial, and development projects in Bangladesh that may have a significant environmental impact are required to obtain an Environmental Clearance Certificate (ECC) from the DoE before commencing operations — with projects classified into four categories (Green, Orange-A, Orange-B, and Red) based on their pollution risk profile.
  • An Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA) is mandatory for Red-category projects (high-pollution industries including tanneries, textile dyeing, chemical manufacturing, and large-scale construction) and for Orange-B category projects above certain thresholds — meaning that many infrastructure and industrial investment projects in Bangladesh require a completed EIA before the DoE will issue environmental clearance.
  • The Environment Court Act, 2000 established dedicated Environment Courts in each district of Bangladesh with exclusive jurisdiction to hear criminal cases involving violations of environmental laws — providing a specialised judicial forum for environmental offences and victim compensation claims.
  • Bangladesh is a signatory to the Paris Agreement, the Convention on Biological Diversity, and numerous other international environmental treaties — obligations under which are increasingly shaping domestic regulatory policy, foreign investment screening, and environmental compliance requirements for international businesses operating in Bangladesh.

Introduction

Bangladesh faces some of the most acute environmental challenges of any country in the world. Densely populated, heavily industrialised in key sectors including garments, textiles, leather, and pharmaceuticals, and acutely vulnerable to climate change — including rising sea levels, increased cyclone frequency, and riverbank erosion — Bangladesh has developed a progressively sophisticated legal framework for environmental protection over the past three decades.

For businesses operating in Bangladesh, environmental law compliance is no longer optional or peripheral. Regulatory enforcement has intensified, international investors and buyers increasingly require environmental compliance certification, and courts have demonstrated a growing willingness to impose significant penalties on industrial polluters. For individuals and communities affected by pollution and environmental damage, the law provides increasingly effective remedies — including compensation and injunctive relief through the Environment Courts.

This article explains Bangladesh's environmental law framework — covering the key statutes, the Environmental Clearance Certificate regime, the EIA requirement, common legal issues for businesses and investors, and how Kamal & Associates provides specialist environmental law advisory and litigation support to clients navigating this increasingly important regulatory landscape.

Legal Framework in Bangladesh

1. The Environment Conservation Act, 1995

The Environment Conservation Act, 1995 (ECA) is the cornerstone of Bangladesh's environmental regulatory system. It prohibits activities that cause or are likely to cause environmental pollution, establishes the regulatory authority of the Department of Environment (DoE), and provides the legal basis for the Environmental Clearance Certificate (ECC) regime. Key provisions include:

  • A general prohibition on pollution of the environment beyond prescribed limits;
  • The requirement for industries and development projects to obtain an ECC before establishment or expansion — classifying projects by pollution risk and imposing correspondingly graduated regulatory requirements;
  • Powers for the DoE to conduct inspections, take environmental samples, issue stop orders, revoke clearances, and initiate criminal prosecution for violations;
  • Criminal penalties including imprisonment of up to ten years and unlimited fines for serious violations — with escalating penalties for repeat offences.

The ECA was amended in 2010 to strengthen enforcement powers and update the penalty regime. The Environment Conservation Rules, 1997 provide the detailed procedural requirements for ECC applications, EIA procedures, environmental standards, and pollution limits for different categories of industry.

2. The Environmental Clearance Certificate (ECC) Regime

Any industrial, commercial, or development project that may have a significant environmental impact must obtain an Environmental Clearance Certificate from the DoE before commencing operations. Projects are classified into four categories:

  • Green category: Lower-risk projects (bookshops, tailoring, small retail) — subject to a simplified clearance process with a site inspection and self-declaration.
  • Orange-A category: Medium-risk projects (flour mills, small workshops, minor construction) — subject to an Initial Environmental Examination (IEE) and standard ECC conditions.
  • Orange-B category: Significant-risk projects (cold storage, medium-scale manufacturing) — subject to more detailed environmental assessment requirements.
  • Red category: High-risk, high-pollution projects (textile dyeing and washing, tanneries, chemical manufacturing, power plants, large-scale construction, shipbreaking) — subject to a full Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA) as a condition of clearance, along with the most rigorous ongoing compliance monitoring.

ECCs must be renewed periodically — annually or every three years depending on the project category. Failure to obtain or renew an ECC, or operating in breach of ECC conditions, is a criminal offence under the ECA.

3. Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA)

An Environmental Impact Assessment is a systematic process for predicting and evaluating the environmental consequences of a proposed project before it is approved and implemented. In Bangladesh, an EIA is mandatory for Red-category projects and for certain Orange-B category undertakings above prescribed thresholds. A compliant EIA must include:

  • A description of the proposed project and its location;
  • A baseline environmental survey of the project area;
  • An assessment of the environmental impacts of the project — including air quality, water quality, soil, biodiversity, and socio-economic impacts on local communities;
  • A mitigation plan identifying measures to minimise or eliminate predicted adverse impacts;
  • An Environmental Management Plan (EMP) for ongoing compliance during construction and operation.

For infrastructure and industrial investment projects — including industrial parks, power plants, roads, bridges, and garment factories — the EIA process is a critical regulatory gateway. A defective or incomplete EIA is a common ground for DoE refusal of ECC applications and can delay or permanently block major investment projects.

4. The Environment Court Act, 2000

The Environment Court Act, 2000 established Environment Courts in each district of Bangladesh with exclusive jurisdiction over criminal cases involving environmental law violations. The Environment Courts hear cases brought by the DoE and by private complainants — including communities and individuals harmed by pollution — and have the power to impose criminal penalties and order compensation for environmental damage. The High Court Division of the Supreme Court exercises appellate jurisdiction over Environment Court decisions and also hears writ petitions challenging administrative decisions by the DoE.

5. Sectoral Environmental Legislation

Beyond the ECA framework, a range of sectoral laws impose specific environmental obligations on particular industries:

  • The Bangladesh Water Act, 2013: Governs the use, conservation, and management of water resources — including river water quality standards and pollution controls.
  • The Forest Act, 1927 (amended): Prohibits deforestation and establishes protected forest areas — with criminal penalties for illegal logging and encroachment.
  • The Wildlife Conservation and Security Act, 2012: Protects biodiversity and wildlife habitats from industrial and development activity, including prohibitions on hunting, trading, and habitat destruction.
  • The Brick Burning (Control) Act, 1989: Regulates brick kiln emissions — a significant source of air pollution in Bangladesh.
  • Bangladesh National Building Code (BNBC): Incorporates environmental standards for construction projects, including energy efficiency and drainage requirements.

Common Legal Issues

1. Failure to Obtain or Renew Environmental Clearance

Operating an industrial or commercial project without a valid Environmental Clearance Certificate is one of the most frequently prosecuted environmental offences in Bangladesh. The DoE has significantly increased inspection and enforcement activity in recent years — particularly in the industrial zones around Dhaka, Chittagong, and Narayanganj. Criminal prosecution can result in closure orders, significant fines, and imprisonment of responsible company directors and officers. Many businesses — including garment factories, tanneries, and food processing plants — operate with expired or inadequate ECCs and face serious legal exposure.

2. Discharge in Excess of Pollution Standards

The Environment Conservation Rules, 1997 prescribe specific discharge standards for air emissions, wastewater effluent, and solid waste for different categories of industry. Industrial operators who discharge pollutants in excess of these standards commit a criminal offence and are exposed to stop orders, fines, and prosecution. Effluent from textile dyeing and tannery operations is a particularly acute problem in Bangladesh, and both the DoE and international buyers now routinely demand effluent treatment plant (ETP) compliance certificates as a condition of continued business.

3. Defective EIA and Project Clearance Disputes

Large infrastructure and industrial investment projects are frequently delayed or blocked by DoE refusals of ECC applications on the basis of incomplete or deficient EIAs. Developers who have invested significantly in site acquisition, design, and project preparation but face DoE rejection require specialist legal advice — including on the grounds for challenging DoE decisions by way of judicial review before the High Court Division, and on rectifying deficiencies in the EIA to resubmit a compliant application.

4. Community and Class Action Environmental Claims

Communities living near industrial facilities — particularly in areas affected by river pollution from factories, air pollution from brick kilns and power plants, and ground contamination from chemical waste — increasingly use the Environment Courts and the High Court Division to seek redress against polluting industries and government bodies that have failed to enforce environmental standards. These proceedings can result in significant compensation awards, closure orders against non-compliant facilities, and mandatory remediation obligations. Industrial operators facing community environmental claims require urgent specialist legal representation.

5. Environmental Due Diligence in Investment and M&A Transactions

Foreign investors acquiring businesses or assets in Bangladesh — particularly in manufacturing, industrial, and real estate sectors — frequently underestimate environmental liability exposure. Acquiring a business with undisclosed environmental violations can transfer criminal liability, remediation obligations, and community compensation claims to the buyer. Comprehensive environmental due diligence — including ECC and EIA compliance review, pollution permit verification, regulatory inspection history, and site contamination assessment — is essential for any investment or acquisition in an environmentally exposed sector.

6. Climate and International Environmental Compliance

Bangladesh's increasing integration with international trade and investment flows has created a new category of environmental compliance obligation: meeting the environmental standards required by international buyers, investors, and multilateral lenders. Garment factories supplying global brands, manufacturers seeking IFC or ADB financing, and infrastructure developers requiring World Bank support all face environmental compliance requirements that go beyond the domestic minimum standards. Navigating the interaction between Bangladesh's domestic environmental regulatory framework and international environmental requirements demands specialist legal and technical expertise.

Practical Tips for Clients

  • Obtain your ECC before commencing operations — without exception: There is no grace period for operating without a valid Environmental Clearance Certificate. The DoE can issue a closure order on the same day as an inspection finding non-compliance. The cost of obtaining an ECC — even for a Red-category project — is a fraction of the cost of defending a criminal prosecution or having a facility forcibly closed.
  • Commission an independent ECC and EIA compliance review annually: As your operations evolve, expand, or introduce new processes, your existing ECC may no longer cover your actual activities. An annual independent review — by an environmental law specialist working with technical consultants — identifies emerging compliance gaps before they become DoE enforcement actions.
  • Install and maintain an Effluent Treatment Plant (ETP) if required: For textile, tannery, and chemical plant operators, an operational and certifiably compliant ETP is not just a regulatory requirement — it is increasingly a commercial prerequisite. International buyers, brand auditors, and export certification bodies now require proof of ETP compliance. An ETP that exists on paper but does not operate effectively creates legal exposure to both DoE prosecution and civil claims from affected communities.
  • Conduct environmental due diligence before acquiring a business or land: Environmental liability in Bangladesh can attach to the current owner of a polluted site regardless of who caused the pollution. Before acquiring a factory, industrial land, or manufacturing business, commission a specialist environmental due diligence review that covers ECC compliance history, DoE inspection records, effluent discharge data, community complaint history, and soil and groundwater contamination risk.
  • Engage with affected communities proactively: Industrial operators whose activities affect neighbouring communities — through noise, emissions, water discharge, or land use — who engage proactively and constructively with community concerns are significantly less likely to face Environment Court proceedings than those who ignore complaints. A structured community engagement programme, addressed through legal counsel, is both a compliance risk management tool and a reputational investment.

The combination of more active DoE inspection programmes, international buyer pressure on supply chains, civil society litigation, and growing judicial assertiveness in the Environment Courts and High Court Division has moved environmental compliance from a peripheral business concern to a core operational and legal risk. FAQs

1. Does my factory or business need an Environmental Clearance Certificate in Bangladesh?
Most industrial and commercial establishments in Bangladesh require an Environmental Clearance Certificate (ECC) from the Department of Environment (DoE) before commencing operations or expanding existing activities. The ECA and the Environment Conservation Rules, 1997 classify projects into four categories (Green, Orange-A, Orange-B, and Red) based on pollution risk. Even relatively small establishments — including workshops, food processing units, and light manufacturing — may fall into a category requiring clearance. Operating without a valid ECC is a criminal offence under the Environment Conservation Act, 1995, and can result in a DoE closure order, substantial fines, and criminal prosecution of responsible individuals. If you are unsure whether your business requires an ECC, or whether your existing ECC covers your current operations, seek specialist legal advice before the DoE raises the question first.

2. What is an Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA) and when is it required?
An Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA) is a formal technical and legal process for predicting, evaluating, and mitigating the environmental impacts of a proposed project before it is approved. In Bangladesh, an EIA is mandatory for Red-category projects — high-pollution industries including tanneries, textile dyeing and washing plants, chemical manufacturing, power plants, shipbreaking yards, and large-scale construction and infrastructure projects. An EIA is also required for certain Orange-B category projects above prescribed thresholds. A compliant EIA must be conducted by a DoE-approved EIA consultant, submitted with the ECC application, and approved by the DoE before environmental clearance is granted. A defective or incomplete EIA is a common reason for DoE refusal of ECC applications for major projects — a refusal that can delay or block significant investment. Specialist legal review of an EIA before DoE submission significantly reduces this risk.

3. What happens if my business is found to be polluting in excess of permitted standards?
If the DoE finds that your business is discharging pollutants — air emissions, wastewater effluent, solid waste — in excess of the standards set by the Environment Conservation Rules, 1997, the consequences can be serious. The DoE can issue a stop notice requiring you to immediately cease the non-compliant activity, revoke your Environmental Clearance Certificate, impose financial penalties, and initiate criminal prosecution in the Environment Court. The Environment Court can impose fines and prison sentences on responsible individuals, as well as ordering remediation and compensation for environmental damage. You should obtain immediate legal representation if you are notified of a DoE inspection finding or enforcement action — the early stages of DoE proceedings are the most important for managing the legal outcome.

4. Can community members or NGOs bring environmental cases in Bangladesh?
Yes. The Environment Court Act, 2000 allows private complainants — including individuals, community groups, and NGOs — to file cases in the Environment Court against industrial operators and government bodies for violations of environmental laws. Additionally, the High Court Division of the Supreme Court of Bangladesh has demonstrated a strong willingness to entertain public interest litigation (PIL) petitions concerning environmental harm — including writ petitions seeking closure of polluting industries, remediation of contaminated rivers and land, and enforcement of environmental standards by the DoE. Several landmark environmental cases in Bangladesh have been initiated by civil society organisations and communities affected by industrial pollution. If you or your community has been harmed by environmental pollution, specialist legal advice will identify the most effective legal route to seek compensation and injunctive relief.

5. How should a foreign investor approach environmental compliance when investing in Bangladesh?
Foreign investors entering Bangladesh — particularly in manufacturing, industrial, and infrastructure sectors — should treat environmental compliance as a pre-investment priority rather than a post-establishment consideration. Before committing capital, conduct a comprehensive environmental due diligence review of any target business, acquired assets, or project site — covering ECC and EIA compliance status, DoE inspection and enforcement history, effluent treatment plant operation, community complaint records, and soil and groundwater contamination risk. At the transaction structuring stage, include environmental representations, warranties, and indemnities in the acquisition agreement to protect against undisclosed pre-acquisition environmental liabilities. Seek specialised legal advice on the environmental compliance requirements applicable to your specific project category and sector — including whether a new ECC or EIA is required on change of ownership or operational expansion. Early-stage environmental legal planning protects both your investment and your international compliance credentials.
