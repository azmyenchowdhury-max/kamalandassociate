Foreign Investment Law in Bangladesh: Rights, Incentives & Legal Framework

Adv. Mohammad Mostofa Kamal
Senior Partner & Managing Advocate, Kamal & Associates
February 7, 2026 11 min read

Primary Keyword: Foreign Investment Bangladesh

Secondary Keywords: FDI Bangladesh • BIDA registration Bangladesh • foreign investor rights Bangladesh • investment incentives Bangladesh • joint venture Bangladesh • repatriation of profits Bangladesh • Foreign Private Investment Act Bangladesh • investment protection Bangladesh

Key Legal Points
  • The Foreign Private Investment (Promotion and Protection) Act, 1980 is the foundational legislation protecting foreign investment in Bangladesh — guaranteeing equal treatment with domestic investors, protection against nationalisation and expropriation without adequate compensation, and the right to repatriate capital and profits in foreign currency.
  • Foreign investors are required to register with the Bangladesh Investment Development Authority (BIDA) — the primary government agency for promoting and facilitating foreign private investment — before commencing business operations, and must obtain sector-specific licences and permits depending on the nature of the investment.
  • Bangladesh permits 100% foreign ownership of companies in most sectors. Exceptions and restrictions apply in certain sectors including banking, insurance, telecommunications, and defence-related industries — where local equity participation requirements or special licensing conditions apply.
  • Bangladesh has concluded bilateral investment treaties (BITs) with over thirty countries, providing foreign investors from those countries with additional treaty-level protections — including fair and equitable treatment, most-favoured-nation treatment, and access to investor-state arbitration for qualifying disputes with the Bangladesh government.
  • Export Processing Zones (EPZs) — administered by the Bangladesh Export Processing Zones Authority (BEPZA) — and Special Economic Zones (SEZs) — administered by the Bangladesh Economic Zones Authority (BEZA) — offer graduated packages of fiscal incentives, streamlined regulatory processes, and dedicated infrastructure for qualifying foreign investments.

Introduction

Bangladesh has emerged as one of South and Southeast Asia's most compelling foreign direct investment destinations. A large and youthful labour force, competitive manufacturing costs, preferential market access to the EU under the Everything But Arms (EBA) scheme and to other major markets under the Generalised System of Preferences (GSP), and a government that has made foreign investment facilitation a stated economic priority — these factors together have attracted significant FDI inflows across garments, textiles, pharmaceuticals, infrastructure, energy, technology, and agribusiness.

For foreign investors considering Bangladesh, however, understanding the legal framework that governs foreign investment — from entry structures and registration requirements through to operational rights, profit repatriation, and dispute resolution — is essential before any capital commitment is made. The legal landscape for foreign investment in Bangladesh is broadly favourable, but it has its complexities: sector-specific restrictions, regulatory approval requirements, foreign exchange controls, and land ownership limitations create legal issues that must be navigated with specialist local counsel.

This article explains the key legal framework governing foreign investment in Bangladesh, the common legal issues foreign investors encounter, and how Kamal & Associates provides the specialist legal advice and structuring support that foreign investors need to enter and operate successfully in Bangladesh.

Legal Framework in Bangladesh

1. The Foreign Private Investment (Promotion and Protection) Act, 1980

The Foreign Private Investment (Promotion and Protection) Act, 1980 (FPIA) is the cornerstone of Bangladesh's foreign investment legal framework. Its key provisions include:

  • Equal treatment: Foreign private investment receives the same treatment as domestic investment — foreign investors are not subject to discriminatory regulation or regulatory conditions more burdensome than those applicable to Bangladesh nationals in the same sector.
  • Protection against nationalisation: Foreign investment is protected against nationalisation or expropriation except under specific statutory authority and subject to the payment of adequate, prompt, and effective compensation.
  • Repatriation rights: Foreign investors have a statutory right to repatriate their invested capital, dividends, profits, and the proceeds of any disposition of their investment in foreign currency — through the authorised dealer banking system subject to Bangladesh Bank guidelines.
  • Settlement of disputes: Investment disputes between the Bangladesh government and a foreign investor may be settled by reference to any method of dispute resolution agreed between the parties — including international arbitration under ICSID or other applicable rules where covered by a bilateral investment treaty.

2. Bangladesh Investment Development Authority (BIDA)

BIDA is the primary government body responsible for promoting, facilitating, and regulating foreign private investment in Bangladesh. Established under the Bangladesh Investment Development Authority Act, 2016 — which merged the former Board of Investment (BOI) and the Privatisation Commission — BIDA provides a one-stop service platform for foreign investors, offering:

  • Registration of foreign investment proposals and issuance of registration certificates;
  • Work permit and visa facilitation for foreign nationals employed in Bangladesh;
  • Facilitation of sector-specific licences, land allocation, utility connections, and other approvals;
  • Dispute mediation services between foreign investors and government agencies;
  • Investment promotion and aftercare services.

BIDA registration is a mandatory prerequisite for foreign investment in most sectors. The registration process requires submission of the investment proposal, company documents, financial projections, and relevant sector-specific documentation. Specialist legal support at the BIDA registration stage significantly reduces processing time and the risk of rejection or conditions.

3. Company Structures for Foreign Investment

Foreign investors may enter Bangladesh through a range of corporate structures established under the Companies Act, 1994:

  • Wholly-owned subsidiary: A private limited company incorporated in Bangladesh with 100% foreign equity — permitted in most sectors. Provides full control and enables full profit repatriation.
  • Joint venture company: A private limited company incorporated in Bangladesh with a combination of foreign and local equity — commonly used where a local partner brings market knowledge, existing relationships, regulatory connections, or sectoral expertise.
  • Branch office: A registered branch of a foreign parent company — permitted for specific categories of business (primarily trading and liaison activities) and requiring BIDA approval. Branch profits are fully repatriable but Bangladesh-source income is taxable in Bangladesh.
  • Liaison or representative office: A non-trading presence used for market research, promotion, and liaison activities only — income-generating operations are not permitted through a liaison office.

The appropriate structure depends on the sector, the nature of the investment, the tax planning objectives, the local partner relationship (if any), and the investor's long-term exit strategy. Each structure has different regulatory, tax, and operational implications that require careful legal analysis before the investment is committed.

4. Investment Incentives — Tax Holidays and Fiscal Benefits

Bangladesh offers a substantial package of fiscal incentives for qualifying foreign investments, administered primarily through the National Board of Revenue (NBR) and the sector-specific investment authorities:

  • Corporate income tax holidays: Qualifying investments in priority sectors — including industrial units outside Dhaka and Chittagong city corporations, hi-tech park tenants, and EPZ/SEZ investors — are eligible for tax exemptions of five to ten years depending on location and sector.
  • Reduced tax rates: Foreign investors in certain sectors benefit from preferential corporate income tax rates below the standard rate.
  • Import duty concessions: Capital machinery and equipment for qualifying industrial investments are importable at concessional or zero customs duty rates.
  • VAT exemptions: Certain inputs and services for export-oriented industries attract VAT exemptions or rebates.
  • EPZ and SEZ incentives: Investors in BEPZA-administered Export Processing Zones and BEZA-administered Special Economic Zones receive the most comprehensive incentive packages — including multi-year tax holidays, duty-free import of capital machinery and raw materials, exemption from certain local taxes and levies, and streamlined regulatory approvals within the zone authority's single-window system.

5. Bilateral Investment Treaties and International Protections

Bangladesh has signed bilateral investment treaties (BITs) with over thirty countries — including the United States, the United Kingdom, Germany, France, the Netherlands, China, India, South Korea, Japan, and the UAE. These BITs provide investors from treaty partner countries with protections that go beyond the domestic FPIA framework, including:

  • Fair and equitable treatment and full protection and security;
  • Most-favoured-nation and national treatment standards;
  • Protection against unlawful expropriation with prompt, adequate, and effective compensation;
  • Free transfer of investment-related payments in freely convertible currency;
  • Access to investor-state arbitration (typically ICSID or UNCITRAL) for qualifying treaty breaches — bypassing Bangladesh's domestic court system.

Foreign investors from BIT partner countries considering significant capital commitments to Bangladesh should obtain specialist advice on the applicable BIT protections as part of their pre-investment legal due diligence.

Common Legal Issues for Foreign Investors

1. Sector Restrictions and Licence Requirements

While Bangladesh broadly permits 100% foreign ownership, certain sectors impose restrictions or require special approvals that are not always clearly communicated at the investment promotion stage. Banking, insurance, and telecommunications require Bangladesh Bank or Bangladesh Telecommunication Regulatory Commission (BTRC) approval and local equity thresholds. Pharmaceutical manufacturing, defence-related supply, and land-intensive activities are subject to additional licensing and ownership conditions. Foreign investors who structure their investment without specialist legal advice on applicable sector restrictions frequently discover compliance problems after capital has been committed — at which point restructuring is costly and disruptive.

2. Land Ownership Restrictions

Foreign companies and individuals cannot directly own agricultural land in Bangladesh. Non-agricultural land — including industrial plots, commercial premises, and residential property — may be acquired by foreign companies incorporated in Bangladesh, but the acquisition process involves multiple regulatory approvals and is more complex than domestic purchases. Most foreign investors address land requirements through long-term lease arrangements (rather than outright purchase) or by acquiring land through a Bangladesh-incorporated wholly-owned subsidiary. EPZ and SEZ investors receive land on a long-term lease from the zone authority — simplifying the land access process considerably.

3. Foreign Exchange and Repatriation Controls

While the FPIA guarantees the right to repatriate capital and profits, the practical mechanics of repatriation involve compliance with Bangladesh Bank's foreign exchange regulations — administered through authorised dealer banks under the Foreign Exchange Regulation Act, 1947. Investors must maintain proper documentation of inward remittances, obtain encashment certificates, and comply with Bangladesh Bank reporting requirements. Failure to maintain compliant records — including the Board of Investment/BIDA registration, inward remittance records, and audited accounts — can impede or delay repatriation applications and attract regulatory scrutiny.

4. Joint Venture Disputes

Joint ventures between foreign and local partners are a common commercial structure in Bangladesh — particularly in sectors where local expertise, regulatory relationships, or distribution networks are important. However, joint venture disputes — including disagreements over management control, profit distribution, expansion decisions, non-compete obligations, and the right to exit or buy out a partner — are among the most commercially disruptive legal problems foreign investors face in Bangladesh. Careful joint venture agreement drafting — covering governance, deadlock resolution, exit rights, and dispute resolution — is essential before any joint venture is committed.

5. Regulatory and Government Interface Challenges

Foreign investors operating in Bangladesh routinely encounter challenges in navigating the regulatory environment — including delays in licence approvals, inconsistent application of regulations across different agencies, unexpected changes in import duty classifications, and difficulties with utility connections for new industrial facilities. While BIDA's one-stop service has improved the investment facilitation environment significantly, specialist legal support — particularly in engaging with regulatory agencies and escalating compliance issues to ministerial level where necessary — remains an important component of a foreign investor's operational legal support framework.

6. Investment Treaty Claims and Dispute Resolution

Where a foreign investor's rights under a bilateral investment treaty have been breached by a Bangladesh government measure — including regulatory changes that affect the value of the investment, arbitrary refusal of permits, discriminatory treatment, or expropriation without adequate compensation — the investor may have the right to initiate investor-state arbitration proceedings under the BIT, typically before ICSID or under UNCITRAL Rules. BIT claims are complex, high-stakes proceedings that require specialist international arbitration counsel with experience in investment treaty law. Early legal advice — before a formal dispute crystallises — is critical to preserving treaty rights and managing the dispute towards the most favourable possible outcome.

Practical Tips for Foreign Investors

  • Engage local legal counsel before committing capital: The most costly foreign investment legal problems in Bangladesh are almost always the product of inadequate legal advice at the pre-investment stage. Structure your investment correctly from the start — including sector restriction analysis, entity selection, and shareholder agreement drafting — rather than attempting to restructure after regulatory problems or partner disputes emerge.
  • Do not underestimate the BIDA registration process: BIDA registration is not a formality — it is a substantive regulatory process with documentation requirements, agency reviews, and conditions. Investors who attempt to self-navigate the BIDA process without specialist legal support frequently experience significant delays, incomplete approvals, or conditions that constrain their operational freedom. Engage experienced BIDA registration counsel from the outset.
  • Conduct thorough legal due diligence on any local partner: Joint venture arrangements with local partners carry inherent risks that need to be assessed through proper legal due diligence before the joint venture agreement is signed. This includes company status and ownership verification, litigation history, regulatory standing, land and asset title searches, and commercial reputation assessment.
  • Understand your BIT protections before investing: If you are investing from a country that has a bilateral investment treaty with Bangladesh — which covers most major investment origin countries — obtain specialist advice on your treaty protections before committing capital. Knowing your BIT rights in advance informs how you structure the investment, what records you maintain, and what legal remedies are available if the investment is harmed by government action.
  • Maintain meticulous foreign exchange documentation from day one: Every inward remittance must be properly documented and encashment certificates obtained. Every dividend repatriation must be supported by audited accounts and compliance with Bangladesh Bank procedures. Building these records from the commencement of operations — rather than attempting to reconstruct them retrospectively when a repatriation application is needed — is essential for frictionless capital and profit repatriation.
Practical Insight

Bangladesh's foreign investment legal framework is substantially more investor-friendly than the country's general regulatory environment sometimes suggests. The FPIA provides strong statutory protections, the BIT network provides international treaty-level guarantees, and Bangladesh's accession to the New York Convention means that arbitral awards obtained against the government are in principle enforceable internationally. The practical challenge for foreign investors is not the inadequacy of the legal framework — it is navigating the gap between the legal rights granted by statute and treaty, and the practical reality of regulatory implementation. That gap is where specialist local legal counsel adds the most decisive value: knowing which agency to engage, which procedure to follow, and when to escalate from administrative channels to judicial or treaty-based remedies. FAQs

1. Can a foreigner own 100% of a company in Bangladesh?
Yes, in most sectors Bangladesh permits 100% foreign equity ownership of companies incorporated under the Companies Act, 1994. There is no general requirement for a local equity partner or local director in most commercial and industrial sectors. However, exceptions apply in specific regulated sectors: banking and financial institutions (governed by the Bank Companies Act, 1991 and Bangladesh Bank regulations) require a specific ownership structure; insurance companies are subject to Insurance Development and Regulatory Authority requirements; telecommunications companies are subject to BTRC licensing conditions that may impose local equity participation requirements; and certain defence-related supply and services are restricted. Before committing to any investment structure, a sector-specific legal assessment is essential to confirm whether any restriction applies to your proposed business activity.

2. What is the process for registering a foreign investment with BIDA?
The BIDA registration process involves several steps: (1) preparation of the investment proposal, including the proposed company structure, shareholding, business activities, capital investment amount, location, employment plan, and financial projections; (2) company incorporation under the Companies Act, 1994, if a new Bangladesh entity is being established; (3) submission of the BIDA registration application with supporting documents — including the incorporation documents, investors' passports or corporate profiles, investment proposal, and sector-specific certificates or NOCs as required; (4) BIDA review and site inspection where applicable; (5) issuance of the BIDA registration certificate and work permit approvals for foreign nationals. The timeline varies by sector and investment complexity — typically four to twelve weeks for straightforward cases. Specialist legal counsel managing the process can significantly reduce delays by ensuring documentation is complete and compliant from submission.

3. Are there any restrictions on repatriating profits from Bangladesh?
The Foreign Private Investment (Promotion and Protection) Act, 1980 confers a statutory right on foreign investors to repatriate invested capital, dividends, profits, and sale proceeds in the freely convertible currency of the investor's choice. Repatriation is processed through authorised dealer banks under Bangladesh Bank guidelines. The practical requirements for repatriation include: maintaining proper documentation of original inward remittances with encashment certificates; filing audited annual financial statements; obtaining board resolutions approving dividend declarations; and complying with Bangladesh Bank reporting and approval procedures. While the legal right to repatriate is clear, investors who have not maintained compliant documentation from the investment's commencement can face delays or procedural obstacles. Specialist legal guidance on foreign exchange compliance from the start of operations is the most reliable way to ensure that repatriation is frictionless when needed.

4. What are Export Processing Zones (EPZs) and are they suitable for my investment?
Export Processing Zones (EPZs) are geographically defined industrial areas in Bangladesh — administered by the Bangladesh Export Processing Zones Authority (BEPZA) — dedicated to export-oriented manufacturing. There are currently eight EPZs in Bangladesh, located in Dhaka, Chittagong, Comilla, Sylhet, Mongla, Ishwardi, Adamjee, and Uttara. EPZ investments receive a comprehensive package of incentives including multi-year corporate income tax holidays, duty-free import of capital machinery and raw materials, exemption from local taxes and levies, streamlined regulatory approvals through BEPZA's single-window system, and dedicated infrastructure and utility supply. EPZ investments must be primarily export-oriented — a significant portion of production must be exported. EPZs are particularly well-suited to garment and textile manufacturers, electronics assembly, light engineering, and pharmaceutical export operations. A similar framework applies to investments in Special Economic Zones (SEZs) administered by BEZA, which offer a slightly broader range of permissible activities. Legal advice on EPZ/SEZ eligibility, investment documentation, and BEPZA/BEZA licensing is advisable before committing to zone-based investment.

5. What legal protections do I have if the Bangladesh government takes action that harms my investment?
Foreign investors in Bangladesh have legal protections at two levels. First, under the Foreign Private Investment (Promotion and Protection) Act, 1980 — which protects against nationalisation and expropriation without adequate compensation and guarantees equal treatment with domestic investors. Disputes under the FPIA can be referred to arbitration by agreement. Second, investors from countries with bilateral investment treaties (BITs) with Bangladesh have treaty-level protections — including fair and equitable treatment, most-favoured-nation treatment, and the right to initiate investor-state arbitration (typically under ICSID or UNCITRAL Rules) for qualifying treaty breaches without needing to exhaust Bangladesh's domestic courts. If you are considering a significant capital commitment to Bangladesh, engaging specialist counsel to review the applicable BIT protections — and to structure your investment in a way that maximises your treaty protection — before the investment is made is among the most valuable legal risk management steps available to you.
