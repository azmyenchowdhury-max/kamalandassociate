Fraud and White Collar Crimes in Bangladesh: Legal Framework, Offences & Defence Strategy

Key Legal Points
  • White collar crimes in Bangladesh are prosecuted under multiple overlapping statutes — principally the Penal Code, 1860 (cheating under Section 415–420, forgery under Section 463–477A, and criminal breach of trust under Section 405–409), the Money Laundering Prevention Act, 2012, the Anti-Corruption Commission Act, 2004, and the Digital Security Act, 2018 for cyber-enabled fraud.
  • The Anti-Corruption Commission (ACC) is constitutionally mandated and investigates corruption and financial crimes involving public officials, banking fraud, and large-scale corporate fraud. ACC investigations can proceed independently of police investigations and carry serious criminal consequences — including imprisonment of up to ten years and unlimited fines.
  • Money laundering under the Money Laundering Prevention Act, 2012 is broadly defined to cover the concealment, transfer, or use of proceeds of any predicate offence — including fraud, corruption, and tax evasion. Conviction carries imprisonment of four to twelve years and confiscation of all proceeds. Bangladesh Bank's Financial Intelligence Unit (BFIU) has extensive surveillance and reporting authority over financial institutions.
  • Accused persons in white collar crime investigations in Bangladesh have constitutionally protected rights — including the right to remain silent, the right to legal representation, and protection against self-incrimination under Articles 35 and 44 of the Constitution of Bangladesh. These rights must be asserted actively; they are not automatically implemented by investigating authorities.
  • White collar crime defendants in Bangladesh frequently face both criminal prosecution and parallel civil proceedings — including cheque dishonour claims under the Negotiable Instruments Act, 1881, civil fraud suits, and regulatory action by the Bangladesh Securities and Exchange Commission (BSEC) or Bangladesh Bank. Managing multiple simultaneous proceedings requires coordinated specialist legal defence.

Introduction

Fraud and white collar crimes represent some of the most serious and complex legal challenges faced by businesses, executives, financial professionals, and individuals in Bangladesh. Unlike violent offences, white collar crimes are non-violent economic offences — typically involving deception, concealment, breach of trust, or manipulation of financial systems for unlawful gain. They range from individual cheating and forgery cases to large-scale corporate fraud, banking fraud, securities manipulation, money laundering, and public corruption.

The consequences of white collar crime accusations in Bangladesh are severe — encompassing criminal prosecution under multiple statutes, substantial prison sentences, unlimited financial penalties, asset freezing and confiscation, regulatory disqualification from business, and reputational destruction. For businesses, white collar crime victimisation — whether through employee fraud, commercial cheating, or financial misrepresentation — can cause devastating financial losses and trigger complex legal recovery processes.

Whether you are facing investigation or prosecution for a white collar offence, defending your business against fraud by a counterparty or employee, or seeking to recover assets lost to financial crime, specialist legal advice from the outset is not optional — it is essential. This article explains the legal framework governing fraud and white collar crimes in Bangladesh, the most common categories of offences and disputes, and how Kamal & Associates provides the specialist criminal defence and commercial litigation expertise that these complex cases demand.

Legal Framework in Bangladesh

1. The Penal Code, 1860 — Core White Collar Offences

The Penal Code, 1860 remains the primary statute for prosecuting fraud and dishonesty-based offences in Bangladesh. The most frequently charged offences include:

  • Cheating (Sections 415–420): Cheating involves deceiving a person dishonestly, causing them to deliver property, consent to the retention of property, or do or omit to do something they would not otherwise do. Section 420 — cheating and dishonestly inducing delivery of property — is among the most commonly charged provisions, carrying imprisonment of up to seven years and a fine. False invoicing, misrepresentation in commercial contracts, fraudulent land transactions, and pyramid scheme promotions are routine Section 420 prosecutions.
  • Forgery (Sections 463–477A): Forgery covers the making of false documents to cause prejudice to another person. It includes fabricating commercial documents, falsifying company accounts, forging cheques and bank instruments, and fraudulently altering legal documents. Forgery with intent to defraud carries up to seven years' imprisonment. Using forged documents carries equivalent penalties.
  • Criminal Breach of Trust (Sections 405–409): This offence applies when a person entrusted with property dishonestly misappropriates, converts, uses, or disposes of it in violation of the terms of the trust. It covers employee embezzlement, company directors misappropriating company funds, trustees defrauding beneficiaries, and agents breaching their financial obligations to principals. Section 409 — breach of trust by a public servant, banker, merchant, factor, broker, attorney, or agent — carries imprisonment of up to ten years and a fine.
  • Criminal Misappropriation of Property (Sections 403–404): Dishonest misappropriation of movable property — including company assets, client funds, or entrusted monies — carries imprisonment of up to two years.

2. The Money Laundering Prevention Act, 2012

The Money Laundering Prevention Act, 2012 (MLPA) is Bangladesh's primary anti-money laundering legislation. It criminalises the acquisition, possession, transfer, concealment, or use of the proceeds of any predicate offence — including fraud, corruption, drug trafficking, or any other cognisable offence. Key features include:

  • Imprisonment of four to twelve years and a fine not less than double the laundered amount — or confiscation of all proceeds if the amount cannot be determined;
  • Broad scope covering individuals, companies, and financial institutions;
  • Mandatory reporting obligations on banks, financial institutions, and money service businesses to Bangladesh Bank's Financial Intelligence Unit (BFIU);
  • BFIU authority to freeze accounts, seize assets, and share intelligence with international financial intelligence units;
  • Proceeds of crime confiscation irrespective of whether the underlying predicate offence is prosecuted separately.

3. The Anti-Corruption Commission Act, 2004

The Anti-Corruption Commission (ACC) — established under the Anti-Corruption Commission Act, 2004 — investigates and prosecutes corruption and financial crimes involving public officials and institutions, as well as large-scale private sector fraud where public interest is implicated. The ACC has independent investigative authority and can initiate prosecution before the Special Judge's Courts established under the Criminal Law Amendment Act, 1958. Corruption convictions carry imprisonment of up to ten years and unlimited fines. The ACC also has jurisdiction over bribery, embezzlement of public funds, abuse of office for financial gain, and procurement fraud.

4. The Digital Security Act, 2018

The Digital Security Act, 2018 (DSA) criminalises fraud committed through digital means — including phishing, identity theft, online fraud, electronic forgery, and non-consensual use of digital signatures or electronic records to deceive. Cyber-enabled financial fraud carries imprisonment of up to fourteen years and substantial fines. The Cyber Tribunal within the Bangladesh court system has jurisdiction over DSA offences.

5. Banking and Securities Regulation

Banking fraud — including loan fraud, trade-based money laundering, fraudulent account opening, and insider trading — is additionally regulated under the Bank Company Act, 1991 and the guidelines of Bangladesh Bank. Securities fraud — including market manipulation, false corporate disclosures, and insider trading — falls within the jurisdiction of the Bangladesh Securities and Exchange Commission (BSEC) under the Securities and Exchange Ordinance, 1969 and the Securities Act, 1993. Both regulators have enforcement powers including civil penalties, business disqualification, and criminal referral to the ACC or police.

Common Legal Issues

1. Commercial Cheating and Contract Fraud

Commercial disputes — including false representation of goods or services, fraudulent advance payments without delivery, dishonoured cheques, and misrepresentation in contracts — frequently give rise to both criminal cheating complaints under Section 420 of the Penal Code and civil fraud suits. The misuse of criminal complaint procedures as leverage in commercial disputes — a well-documented phenomenon in Bangladesh's legal landscape — means that businesses and executives can face criminal proceedings arising from what are, in substance, contractual disagreements. Specialist legal advice is essential both to defend wrongful criminal complaints and to pursue legitimate fraud recovery through both criminal and civil channels.

2. Employee and Internal Fraud

Internal fraud — including embezzlement by employees, payroll fraud, procurement kickbacks, false expense claims, and theft of business assets — is a significant problem for businesses of all sizes in Bangladesh. Prosecuting internal fraud requires careful preservation of electronic and documentary evidence, forensic accounting analysis, and simultaneous civil recovery proceedings alongside any criminal complaint. Businesses that self-investigate without specialist legal guidance frequently compromise both the criminal case and their prospects of asset recovery.

3. Banking and Loan Fraud

Bangladesh's banking sector has experienced significant problems with fraudulent loan applications, inflated asset valuations in collateral submissions, falsified trade documents in letter of credit transactions, and loan proceeds diversion. These cases typically involve parallel investigations by the ACC, Bangladesh Bank's inspection department, and the Criminal Investigation Department (CID) of Bangladesh Police. Multiple agencies with overlapping jurisdiction create complex procedural situations that require specialist criminal defence counsel with experience of the Bangladesh financial crime investigation environment.

4. Corporate and Securities Fraud

Corporate fraud — including false financial reporting, fraudulent prospectus disclosures, insider trading, and market price manipulation — is investigated by the BSEC and can result in criminal referral to the Special Judge's Courts. Company directors and officers who are the subject of BSEC investigations face personal criminal liability and business disqualification in addition to civil regulatory penalties. Prompt engagement of specialist legal counsel at the investigation stage — before formal charges are filed — is the most important step available to corporate fraud suspects in managing both the criminal exposure and the regulatory consequences.

5. Money Laundering and Asset Freezing

Money laundering investigations in Bangladesh — initiated by the ACC, BFIU, or CID — frequently involve emergency asset freezing orders that can be devastating to both personal and business financial stability while investigations proceed. Bangladesh's anti-money laundering framework allows assets to be frozen on relatively low evidential thresholds at the investigation stage. Challenging freeze orders and managing the consequential financial disruption requires urgent specialist legal action — the window for effective early intervention is narrow.

Practical Tips for Clients

  • Exercise your right to silence immediately: If you are called for questioning by the ACC, CID, or any investigating authority in connection with a suspected fraud offence, you have a constitutional right not to incriminate yourself. Do not answer questions without first speaking to a specialist criminal defence lawyer. Statements made voluntarily — even informally — can be used against you.
  • Do not destroy or alter documents: Destroying, altering, or concealing documents once you know an investigation is underway — or reasonably should know — constitutes a separate criminal offence of tampering with evidence. Preserve all documents, communications, and electronic records, and put your legal counsel in control of document management immediately.
  • Engage specialist counsel before criminal complaints are filed: If you are aware that a counterparty or disgruntled employee is threatening to file a criminal fraud complaint, engaging specialist legal counsel immediately — before the complaint is filed — significantly improves your position. Early legal intervention can sometimes prevent unmeritorious complaints from being filed or can ensure that the investigation proceeds within appropriate procedural boundaries.
  • For businesses — implement an internal fraud response protocol: When internal fraud is discovered, preserve electronic and documentary evidence immediately (engage IT forensics if necessary), do not confront the suspected employee before taking legal advice on the consequences for the criminal and civil case, and instruct specialist criminal and employment law counsel before taking any further action.
  • Act urgently on asset freeze orders: A BFIU or court-ordered asset freeze can be challenged — but the procedural windows for challenge are short. If your accounts or assets have been frozen in connection with a money laundering or fraud investigation, contact specialist legal counsel on the same day you receive notice of the freeze.

FAQs

1. What is the difference between cheating and fraud under Bangladesh law?
In Bangladesh's legal system, "fraud" is the general English term commonly used to describe a range of dishonesty-based offences — while the Penal Code, 1860 specifically uses the term "cheating" (Sections 415–420). Cheating under the Penal Code requires dishonest deception — the accused must have induced a person to act to their detriment by misrepresentation or fraudulent concealment. Section 420 — the most commonly charged provision — covers situations where the deception caused the victim to deliver property, and carries imprisonment of up to seven years. Other related offences in the same conceptual family include forgery, criminal breach of trust (which covers situations where property was entrusted rather than obtained by deception), and criminal misappropriation. The precise offence charged depends on the method by which the dishonest advantage was obtained, and specialist advice on which provisions apply to a specific situation is important for both prosecution strategy (for victims) and defence preparation (for accused persons).

2. Can a business dispute result in a criminal fraud case in Bangladesh?
Yes — and this is a significant and well-known feature of Bangladesh's legal landscape. Commercial disputes, including non-payment of contractual obligations, failure to deliver goods or services after advance payment, and breakdown of business partnerships, frequently give rise to criminal complaints under Section 420 of the Penal Code. Bangladesh law does not require that a criminal complaint be the sole remedy for a commercial dispute, and parties sometimes file criminal complaints in parallel with civil proceedings — either as a legitimate fraud recovery strategy or as litigation pressure. The result is that business executives and company directors can face criminal proceedings arising from commercial transactions that the accused genuinely regard as contractual disputes rather than fraud. Defending against wrongful criminal criminalisation of commercial disputes is a distinct and important area of Bangladesh's criminal defence practice, and specialist counsel with experience of both criminal and commercial law is essential in these situations.

3. What should I do if I receive notice of an ACC investigation?
If you receive notice that the Anti-Corruption Commission (ACC) has initiated an investigation in which you are named as a suspect or witness, you should engage specialist criminal defence counsel immediately — before responding to the ACC notice or attending any ACC inquiry. The ACC has broad investigative powers and the capacity to file charges that carry imprisonment of up to ten years. Your constitutional rights include the right to legal representation at all stages of ACC proceedings, the right to remain silent, and protection against self-incrimination. However, these rights must be asserted actively — they are not automatically protected by the investigation process. A specialist criminal defence lawyer will advise you on how to respond to ACC notices, what documents — if any — you are legally required to produce, how to manage the investigation at the inquiry stage before formal charges are brought, and whether anticipatory bail or other protective measures are appropriate given your specific circumstances.

4. How does Bangladesh's money laundering framework affect businesses?
Bangladesh's Money Laundering Prevention Act, 2012 (MLPA) affects businesses in two important ways. First, all financial institutions operating in Bangladesh — including banks, non-bank financial institutions, insurance companies, and money service businesses — are subject to mandatory anti-money laundering (AML) compliance obligations, including customer due diligence (KYC), transaction monitoring, and suspicious transaction reporting (STR) to Bangladesh Bank's Financial Intelligence Unit (BFIU). Failure to comply with AML obligations exposes institutions and their responsible officers to regulatory penalties and criminal prosecution. Second, any business — regardless of sector — whose operations involve the proceeds of predicate crimes committed by customers, counterparties, or employees can face money laundering exposure if the business "ought to have known" that the funds were criminal proceeds. This means that robust AML compliance frameworks and proper legal advice on high-risk transactions are important risk management tools for businesses operating in Bangladesh's economy.

5. What is the process for recovering assets lost to fraud in Bangladesh?
Asset recovery following fraud in Bangladesh involves a combination of criminal and civil processes. On the criminal side, filing a complaint with the police (typically with the Criminal Investigation Department for complex fraud cases) or the ACC initiates an investigation that can lead to asset seizure and confiscation — with confiscated proceeds in some cases available for return to victims. On the civil side, victims can seek emergency civil court injunctions to freeze the fraudster's assets, file civil suits for damages and fraudulent conversion, initiate Negotiable Instruments Act proceedings for cheque dishonour, and pursue tracing claims to asset transfers. The most effective recovery strategy typically combines both approaches — with the criminal process creating investigative capacity (particularly for obtaining financial records and compelling disclosure) while the civil process enables victim-controlled asset freezing and direct court enforcement. Specialist legal counsel experienced in both criminal fraud prosecution and commercial litigation asset recovery is essential to coordinate this dual-track approach effectively in Bangladesh's court system.
