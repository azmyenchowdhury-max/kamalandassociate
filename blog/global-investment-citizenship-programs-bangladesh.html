Global Investment & Citizenship Programs: A Legal Guide for Bangladeshi Investors

Senior Partner & Managing Advocate, Kamal & Associates

Key Legal Points
  • Bangladesh does not generally permit dual citizenship for adult Bangladeshi nationals by birth — the Bangladesh Citizenship (Temporary Provisions) Order, 1972 and the Bangladesh Citizenship Act, 1951 provide that acquiring the citizenship of another country ordinarily results in the loss of Bangladeshi citizenship. However, the government has established a Non-Resident Bangladeshi (NRB) status framework that allows Bangladeshis residing abroad to retain specific rights and privileges even after acquiring foreign nationality — including the right to invest, hold property, and operate businesses in Bangladesh.
  • Bangladeshi nationals who acquire citizenship or permanent residency in another country through an investment immigration programme must comply with Bangladesh Bank's foreign exchange regulations governing the outward remittance of capital for overseas investment — including obtaining prior Bangladesh Bank approval for investments above prescribed threshold amounts under the Foreign Exchange Regulation Act, 1947.
  • Global citizenship-by-investment (CBI) and residency-by-investment (RBI) programmes — offered by countries including Malta, Portugal, Greece, the UAE, Canada, several Caribbean nations, and others — have distinct investment thresholds, due diligence requirements, processing timelines, and legal consequences that require careful evaluation against the investor's personal objectives, Bangladesh-law obligations, and international tax planning considerations.
  • Anti-money laundering (AML) and source-of-funds due diligence requirements in global CBI and RBI programmes have become significantly more rigorous following international regulatory scrutiny. Bangladeshi investors applying to these programmes must be prepared to demonstrate the legitimate origin of their investment funds through comprehensive documentation — a process that requires advance legal preparation of asset and income history files.
  • The legal and tax consequences of acquiring foreign citizenship or residency — including potential changes to tax residency status, cross-border estate planning, the impact on Bangladesh asset ownership and repatriation rights, and continued obligations under Bangladesh law — require comprehensive specialist legal and tax advice before any application is submitted.

Introduction

Global investment and citizenship programmes — commonly known as citizenship-by-investment (CBI) or residency-by-investment (RBI) schemes, and frequently referred to by the colloquial term "Golden Visa" — have become one of the most significant cross-border legal and financial planning tools available to high-net-worth individuals and families worldwide. For Bangladeshi investors, business families, and Non-Resident Bangladeshis (NRBs), these programmes offer a range of strategic benefits: greater global mobility through visa-free or visa-on-arrival travel access, business and educational opportunities in third countries, personal and family security diversification, and in some cases more favourable international tax environments.

Interest in global investment and citizenship programmes among Bangladeshi investors and business families has grown substantially over the past decade. However, navigating these programmes from a Bangladesh legal perspective involves a set of distinct legal questions — from Bangladesh's constitutional provisions on citizenship and the rules governing dual nationality, through to Bangladesh Bank's foreign exchange regulations on outward capital remittance, the source-of-funds documentation requirements of overseas programme administrators, and the Bangladesh tax and estate planning consequences of acquiring foreign nationality or residency status.

This article explains the key legal framework that Bangladeshi investors must understand before pursuing global investment and citizenship programmes, the common legal issues that arise in practice, and how Kamal & Associates provides the specialist legal advisory services that these complex cross-border matters require.

Legal Framework in Bangladesh

1. Bangladesh Citizenship Law — Dual Nationality Rules

Bangladesh's primary citizenship legislation — the Bangladesh Citizenship Act, 1951, as amended, and the Bangladesh Citizenship (Temporary Provisions) Order, 1972 — establishes the rules governing the acquisition, retention, and loss of Bangladeshi citizenship. Under this framework:

  • A Bangladeshi citizen who voluntarily acquires the citizenship of another country generally loses their Bangladeshi citizenship automatically upon such acquisition — unless the government grants a specific exemption or the dual nationality is covered by a bilateral agreement.
  • Bangladeshi-origin individuals who have already acquired foreign nationality may hold a dual citizenship certificate or a Special Certificate issued by the Bangladesh government in specific circumstances — though the conditions for such certificates are not universally applicable and require individual assessment.
  • Bangladeshi nationals who acquire permanent residency (rather than citizenship) in a foreign country do not automatically lose their Bangladeshi citizenship — permanent residency abroad does not trigger the citizenship loss provisions of Bangladesh law.

The threshold consequence of this framework is that Bangladeshi investors considering citizenship-by-investment programmes — which result in the grant of full foreign citizenship — must carefully evaluate whether and how Bangladesh citizenship law applies to their situation, and what the consequences of Bangladeshi citizenship loss would be for their Bangladesh-based asset holdings, business interests, and family arrangements.

2. Non-Resident Bangladeshi (NRB) Status

The Bangladesh government has established a Non-Resident Bangladeshi (NRB) framework — administered through the Ministry of Expatriates' Welfare and Overseas Employment and the relevant Bangladesh missions abroad — that recognises the distinct status of Bangladeshis residing and working overseas, including those who have acquired foreign nationality. NRB status provides holders with specific entitlements in Bangladesh including:

  • The right to invest in Bangladesh businesses, including through dedicated NRB investment incentive schemes administered by BIDA;
  • Access to NRB savings accounts and foreign currency deposit accounts in Bangladesh's banking system;
  • The right to hold and acquire immovable property (land and buildings) in Bangladesh — subject to applicable land ownership laws;
  • Facilitated Bangladesh visa and travel documentation for Bangladeshi-origin foreign nationals;
  • Access to NRB bond and securities investment schemes offering preferential returns.

For Bangladeshi investors who have acquired or are planning to acquire foreign nationality, NRB status is typically the mechanism through which their connectivity to Bangladesh's economy and legal system is maintained. Specialist legal advice on NRB documentation, rights, and compliance obligations is an important part of any investment immigration planning process involving Bangladesh-origin investors.

3. Foreign Exchange Regulation — Outward Capital Remittance

The outward remittance of capital from Bangladesh for overseas investment — including for the purpose of funding citizenship-by-investment or residency-by-investment programme applications — is regulated under the Foreign Exchange Regulation Act, 1947 (FERA) and the guidelines issued by Bangladesh Bank. Key requirements include:

  • Prior Bangladesh Bank approval: Remittances of capital for foreign direct investment by Bangladesh residents require prior approval from Bangladesh Bank above specified thresholds. The approval process requires documentation of the investment purpose, the overseas investee entity, the investor's source of funds, and the investor's compliance history with Bangladesh foreign exchange regulations.
  • Authorised dealer bank facilitation: All outward capital remittances must be processed through authorised dealer banks in Bangladesh — which are responsible for verifying the supporting documentation and Bangladesh Bank approval before effecting the transfer.
  • Reporting obligations: Bangladeshi investors remitting capital overseas are subject to ongoing reporting obligations to Bangladesh Bank regarding the status and returns of their overseas investments.

Failure to comply with FERA and Bangladesh Bank requirements for outward investment remittances can constitute a criminal offence under Bangladesh law — making advance legal clearance of the proposed remittance mechanism an essential step before any overseas investment programme application is funded.

4. Overview of Leading Global Investment Immigration Programmes

The most frequently considered global investment and citizenship programmes by Bangladeshi investors include a range of regional options:

  • Caribbean citizenship-by-investment programmes: Antigua and Barbuda, Dominica, Grenada, Saint Kitts and Nevis, and Saint Lucia all operate established CBI programmes offering full citizenship and passports — with visa-free access to a large number of countries — in exchange for qualifying real estate investment or contributions to government funds. Minimum investment thresholds range from approximately USD 100,000 to USD 200,000 depending on the programme and investment route. Processing timelines are typically three to six months.
  • European residency-by-investment (Golden Visa) programmes: Several EU member states — including Greece and Malta — continue to operate residency or citizenship-by-investment programmes offering EU residency rights and, in Malta's case, full EU citizenship, in exchange for real estate, fund, or government bond investments. Thresholds range from EUR 250,000 to over EUR 700,000 depending on the country and investment category. EU residency programmes offer the significant benefit of access to EU Schengen area visa-free travel and eventual EU citizenship eligibility for qualifying long-term residents.
  • UAE Golden Visa: The UAE's Golden Visa programme offers long-term residency (five or ten years, renewable) for qualifying investors, entrepreneurs, professionals, and property owners. Real estate investment of AED 2 million qualifies for the ten-year Golden Visa. The UAE is particularly attractive to Bangladeshi business families with existing Gulf trading connections and offers a zero personal income tax environment.
  • Canada and other common law jurisdiction programmes: Canada's various immigration programmes including the Start-Up Visa, Provincial Nominee Programs (PNPs), and the federal investor immigrant stream (where available) offer permanent residency pathways for qualifying investors and entrepreneurs. These programmes have different legal and lifestyle implications from pure CBI programmes, and are typically more appropriate for investors with long-term settlement intentions rather than those seeking primarily mobility and tax planning benefits.

Common Legal Issues

1. Bangladesh Citizenship Loss Risk

The most significant Bangladesh-specific legal risk in citizenship-by-investment programmes is the potential automatic loss of Bangladeshi citizenship upon acquiring a foreign nationality. Many Bangladeshi investors who apply for CBI programmes do not obtain specialist Bangladesh legal advice before their foreign citizenship is granted — and discover afterwards that their Bangladeshi passport is no longer valid, that their right to own property in Bangladesh has been affected, and that their existing business shareholdings may require restructuring. Advance legal advice on Bangladesh citizenship law consequences — and on whether NRB status adequately protects the investor's Bangladesh interests — is essential before any CBI application is committed.

2. Source-of-Funds Documentation Failures

CBI and RBI programme administrators worldwide — and particularly those in EU jurisdictions and Caribbean nations operating under enhanced international scrutiny — conduct rigorous anti-money laundering (AML) due diligence on investment funds. Bangladeshi investors frequently face challenges in documenting the legitimate origin of their investment capital to the satisfaction of overseas programme administrators — particularly where wealth has been accumulated over many years in an environment where formal documentation of asset acquisition history and business income is incomplete. Advance preparation of a comprehensive source-of-funds file — including audited business accounts, land and property valuation evidence, bank statements, and tax compliance records — is critical to a successful application.

3. Bangladesh Bank Remittance Non-Compliance

Investors who fund overseas CBI or RBI programme investments through informal money transfer channels — or who remit funds without obtaining required Bangladesh Bank approvals — expose themselves to serious criminal liability under the Foreign Exchange Regulation Act, 1947 and the Money Laundering Prevention Act, 2012. The Bangladesh Bank and the Anti-Corruption Commission (ACC) have both become more active in scrutinising large overseas capital transfers. Structuring overseas investment remittances through proper Bangladesh Bank-approved channels — with full documentation — is both a legal obligation and a practical necessity for maintaining the investor's ability to continue doing business in Bangladesh.

4. Cross-Border Tax Complications

Acquiring foreign citizenship or tax residency through an investment immigration programme can have significant international tax consequences — particularly for investors with substantial Bangladesh-source income and assets. Bangladesh's National Board of Revenue (NBR) taxes Bangladeshi residents on their worldwide income. Acquiring non-resident status through extended overseas residence or foreign citizenship can change an investor's Bangladesh tax residency status with significant implications for both Bangladesh tax obligations and the tax treatment of income repatriated to or received in the new country of citizenship or residency. Specialist legal and tax advice on the cross-border tax consequences — including applicable double taxation agreements between Bangladesh and the target country — is essential before finalising any investment immigration strategy.

Practical Tips for Clients

  • Get Bangladesh legal advice before, not after, applying: The sequence of legal advice matters enormously in investment immigration planning. Many Bangladeshi investors engage overseas immigration consultants first and receive Bangladesh legal advice — if at all — only after the foreign citizenship has already been granted. This sequence risks irreversible Bangladesh citizenship loss, property rights complications, and foreign exchange violations that are significantly more difficult and costly to address retrospectively. Engage Bangladesh specialist legal counsel as the first step in your investment immigration planning.
  • Start your source-of-funds documentation early: Compiling a credible and comprehensive source-of-funds file for a CBI or RBI application typically takes several months of document collection and organisation — particularly where income and asset acquisition have not been systematically documented over many years. Begin this process well before your intended application submission date, and engage legal counsel experienced in preparing these files for Bangladeshi applicants.
  • Use only Bangladesh Bank-approved remittance channels: Funding an overseas investment immigration application through hawala, informal transfers, or any channel that circumvents Bangladesh Bank's foreign exchange approval requirements is a serious criminal offence in Bangladesh. No investment immigration benefit is worth the risk of FERA or Money Laundering Prevention Act prosecution. Invest the time and effort required to secure proper Bangladesh Bank approval for the capital remittance — your specialist legal counsel can manage this process for you.
  • Assess the programme against your actual objectives: Different investment immigration programmes serve different objectives. If your primary goal is global travel mobility, a Caribbean CBI programme may be most efficient. If EU residency and eventual EU citizenship are important, a European Golden Visa programme with a path to permanent residency is more appropriate. If tax efficiency in a zero-tax jurisdiction is the priority, the UAE Golden Visa merits assessment alongside the associated residency and lifestyle requirements. Expert legal and lifestyle planning advice — covering both the Bangladesh and international dimensions — leads to much more satisfying long-term outcomes than selecting a programme based purely on marketing materials or peer recommendation.
  • Plan your estate and succession alongside investment immigration: Acquiring foreign citizenship or residency has significant estate planning implications — particularly for investors with Bangladesh immovable property, family business interests, and assets in multiple jurisdictions. Combine your investment immigration planning with a review of your Bangladesh will, any existing trust arrangements, and your cross-border succession strategy to ensure that your estate plan remains coherent and legally effective across all relevant jurisdictions.

FAQs

1. Will I lose my Bangladeshi citizenship if I acquire foreign citizenship through a CBI programme?
Under the Bangladesh Citizenship Act, 1951 and the Bangladesh Citizenship (Temporary Provisions) Order, 1972, voluntarily acquiring the citizenship of another country generally results in the automatic loss of Bangladeshi citizenship. This rule applies regardless of how the foreign citizenship is acquired — including through a citizenship-by-investment programme. There is no general dual citizenship framework in Bangladesh that would allow you to retain Bangladeshi citizenship after acquiring a foreign nationality. The practical consequences of citizenship loss include: invalidation of your Bangladeshi passport, potential complications with Bangladesh property and business ownership rights, and changes to your tax residency status. However, this does not mean that your connection to Bangladesh must be severed. Non-Resident Bangladeshi (NRB) status provides Bangladeshi-origin foreign nationals with a range of rights — including the right to invest, hold property, and operate businesses in Bangladesh. Specialist legal advice before your foreign citizenship is granted allows you to plan the transition carefully and ensure that your Bangladesh interests are protected under the NRB framework.

2. What is Non-Resident Bangladeshi (NRB) status and how does it help?
Non-Resident Bangladeshi (NRB) is a legal status recognised by the Bangladesh government that applies to Bangladeshi nationals and persons of Bangladeshi origin who are residing abroad — including those who have acquired foreign citizenship. NRB status entitles holders to invest in Bangladesh under dedicated NRB investment schemes (offering preferential terms compared to general foreign investor conditions), hold NRB bank accounts and foreign currency accounts in Bangladesh, own and acquire immovable property in Bangladesh, participate in NRB savings bond and securities schemes, and travel to Bangladesh on a facilitated visa basis. NRB status is not automatic — it requires registration with the relevant Bangladesh government authorities through the Bangladesh mission in your country of residence. Maintaining valid NRB status is the primary mechanism through which Bangladeshi-origin investors who have acquired foreign nationality preserve their legal connection to Bangladesh's economy and property market. Specialist legal counsel can assist with NRB registration documentation and ongoing compliance.

3. Do I need Bangladesh Bank approval to fund an overseas investment immigration application?
Yes — for most investment immigration programme funding amounts, prior Bangladesh Bank approval is required for the outward remittance of capital from Bangladesh under the Foreign Exchange Regulation Act, 1947. Bangladesh Bank permits certain categories of outward remittance for overseas investment by Bangladesh residents above specified thresholds only with prior approval, and the approval process requires documentation of the investment purpose, the overseas investee entity, and the source of the funds being remitted. Investors who transfer funds overseas without obtaining required Bangladesh Bank approvals — regardless of the method of transfer — risk prosecution under the FERA and, if the transfer involves proceeds of unlawful activity, under the Money Laundering Prevention Act, 2012. The right approach is to engage Bangladesh specialist legal counsel to assess the applicable Bangladesh Bank requirements for your specific investment amount and programme, prepare the necessary application documentation, and manage the approval process through an authorised dealer bank before any funds are transferred.

4. Which citizenship-by-investment programme is best for Bangladeshi investors?
There is no single "best" programme — the most appropriate choice depends on your specific objectives, investment capacity, travel requirements, family circumstances, timeline, and tax planning considerations. Caribbean CBI programmes (Dominica, Grenada, Saint Kitts and Nevis, Antigua and Barbuda) offer relatively affordable entry points (from approximately USD 100,000–200,000) and fast processing (three to six months), but the passports offer more limited visa-free access than EU programmes. European Golden Visa programmes offer EU residency and a path to EU citizenship but require significantly higher investment thresholds (EUR 250,000 to EUR 700,000 and above) and longer timelines to citizenship eligibility. The UAE Golden Visa offers long-term residency (not citizenship) in a zero-tax environment that is familiar and accessible to many Bangladeshi business families. Each programme also introduces different Bangladesh-law consequences — particularly regarding citizenship loss, NRB eligibility, and tax residency — that must be assessed as part of the selection process. Kamal & Associates can provide a comparative legal and practical assessment of the programmes most relevant to your situation, covering both the overseas programme requirements and the Bangladesh legal implications.

5. Can a Bangladeshi investor own property in Bangladesh after acquiring foreign citizenship?
Yes — but the manner and legal basis of ownership changes after Bangladeshi citizenship is lost. Bangladeshis who acquire foreign citizenship and lose their Bangladeshi citizenship are no longer entitled to own property in Bangladesh as citizens. However, Non-Resident Bangladeshi (NRB) status enables Bangladeshi-origin foreign nationals to own and acquire immovable property in Bangladesh — including residential and commercial properties — under the applicable NRB property ownership rules. Existing properties owned at the time of naturalisation in a foreign country do not automatically become invalid; however, the legal title and ownership structure may need to be reviewed and in some cases adjusted to ensure continued compliance with Bangladesh property and land ownership laws as they apply to foreign nationals of Bangladeshi origin. Advance legal review of existing property holdings — and planning for any restructuring that may be required — before the foreign citizenship application is completed is strongly recommended.
