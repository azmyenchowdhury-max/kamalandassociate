Government Contracts & Litigation in Bangladesh: Legal Rights, Remedies & Strategy for Contractors

Key Legal Points
  • Government contracts in Bangladesh are primarily governed by the Public Procurement Act, 2006 (PPA) and the Public Procurement Rules, 2008 (PPR) — which regulate the entire procurement cycle from tender publication through contract award, execution, variation, payment, and termination. All government ministries, departments, statutory bodies, and public corporations are required to conduct procurement in compliance with the PPA and PPR framework administered by the Central Procurement Technical Unit (CPTU).
  • Where a government procuring entity acts unlawfully, arbitrarily, or in breach of the PPA/PPR framework in its procurement or contract administration decisions — including wrongful disqualification of tender bids, procedurally defective contract awards, unlawful blacklisting of contractors, or refusal to release payment of undisputed amounts — contractors and tenderers have the right to seek judicial review through a writ petition to the High Court Division under Article 102 of the Constitution of Bangladesh.
  • The PPA, 2006 establishes a mandatory administrative review mechanism — the Review Panel process — for challenging procurement decisions before they crystallise into final contract awards. Contractors and tenderers who fail to exhaust the PPA Review Panel process before approaching the courts risk having their legal proceedings dismissed on the ground of failure to exhaust administrative remedies.
  • Disputes arising during the execution of government contracts — including payment disputes, variation order valuation disputes, force majeure claims, and termination disputes — are typically subject to the dispute resolution provisions of the specific contract, which under the PPA/PPR framework are usually the MDB Harmonised Conditions of Contract (for international contracts) or the Bangladesh Standard Bidding Document conditions (for domestic contracts), and ultimately to the jurisdiction of Bangladesh's civil courts.
  • Contractors blacklisted by a government procuring entity under Rule 127 of the PPR, 2008 are entitled to receive prior notice and an opportunity to be heard before the blacklisting decision takes effect — the failure of a procuring entity to follow this procedural requirement renders the blacklisting decision susceptible to challenge and quashing by the High Court Division on constitutional grounds.

Introduction

Government contracts represent one of the largest and most commercially important categories of contractual work available to businesses, contractors, suppliers, and consultants operating in Bangladesh. The Bangladesh government — at the national, divisional, and local levels — procures an enormous volume of works, goods, and services annually: infrastructure construction, road maintenance, power generation equipment, IT systems, healthcare supplies, educational materials, and a vast range of professional consultancy services. For businesses engaged in the public sector market, government contracts are major revenue drivers — but they also carry distinctive legal risks and challenges that differ fundamentally from private commercial contracts.

The legal relationship between a government contractor and a public body in Bangladesh is governed not only by the terms of the specific contract, but also by the PPA and PPR framework, the general law of contract under the Contract Act, 1872, the constitutional principles of good governance and administrative law, and — increasingly — by Bangladesh's judiciary's willingness to scrutinise government procurement decisions for legality and procedural fairness. Contractors, tenderers, and suppliers who understand this legal landscape and who engage specialist legal counsel are significantly better positioned to protect their commercial interests — whether in pursuing payment, challenging an unfair tender disqualification, resisting unlawful blacklisting, or recovering damages for wrongful contract termination.

This article explains the legal framework governing government contracts and procurement litigation in Bangladesh, the most common legal issues that arise in practice, and how Kamal & Associates provides the specialist legal support that government contractors and public sector suppliers require.

Legal Framework in Bangladesh

1. The Public Procurement Act, 2006

The Public Procurement Act, 2006 (PPA) is the primary legislation governing all public procurement in Bangladesh. Its key provisions establish:

  • The mandatory application of the PPA framework to all procurements by government ministries, departments, divisions, districts, statutory bodies, and public corporations — regardless of the source of funding (government budget or donor-financed);
  • Core procurement principles of transparency, competition, fairness, accountability, and value for money that bind all procuring entities in their procurement activities;
  • The Review Panel mechanism — which provides a formal administrative challenge process for tenderers who believe that a procurement decision is non-compliant with the PPA/PPR framework, allowing challenges to be raised and decided before final contract award;
  • The authority of CPTU — the Central Procurement Technical Unit under the Implementation Monitoring and Evaluation Division of the Ministry of Planning — to monitor, regulate, and provide guidance on public procurement practice across all government entities.

2. The Public Procurement Rules, 2008

The Public Procurement Rules, 2008 (PPR) provide the detailed procedural framework implementing the PPA — covering every stage of the procurement process including:

  • Procurement planning and budget approval requirements;
  • Tender document preparation and publication obligations — including mandatory use of CPTU-issued Standard Bidding Documents for different procurement categories;
  • Bid opening, evaluation, and award procedures — including the criteria and process for responsive/non-responsive bid determinations;
  • Contract signing, performance security requirements, and advance payment conditions;
  • Variation order procedures — including approval thresholds for variations above specified percentages of the original contract value;
  • Contractor performance assessment and blacklisting procedures under Rule 127;
  • Contract completion, defects liability, and final payment procedures.

3. The Contract Act, 1872

Government contracts are also subject to the general law of contract under the Contract Act, 1872 — including the essential requirements of offer, acceptance, consideration, and lawful object for a binding contract, the rules governing breach of contract and remedies including damages, and the principles applicable to force majeure, frustration, and impossibility of performance. The Contract Act provides the foundational legal framework within which the specific PPA/PPR terms operate, and its provisions on contractual remedies — including damages, specific performance, and restitution — are available to government contractors in appropriate cases.

4. Constitutional Remedies — Article 102 Writ Jurisdiction

The High Court Division of the Supreme Court of Bangladesh has constitutional jurisdiction under Article 102 of the Constitution to review the decisions and actions of government bodies — including procuring entities — for legality, procedural fairness, and compliance with the principles of natural justice. This writ jurisdiction provides government contractors and tenderers with a powerful legal remedy that goes beyond the contractual rights available under the specific contract:

  • Certiorari: To quash an unlawful government procurement decision — such as a wrongful tender disqualification, an arbitrary contract termination, or an unlawfully imposed blacklisting;
  • Mandamus: To compel a government body to perform a legal duty — such as processing a payment that it is legally obliged to make, completing a tender evaluation in accordance with the prescribed process, or issuing a completion certificate to which a contractor is entitled;
  • Injunction: To restrain a government body from implementing an unlawful procurement decision — such as awarding a contract to a non-compliant tenderer or proceeding with an unlawful blacklisting — pending judicial review.

Common Legal Issues

1. Wrongful Tender Disqualification and Non-Responsive Findings

One of the most common and commercially significant problems faced by government contractors in Bangladesh is the arbitrary or procedurally defective disqualification of tender bids. Procuring entities sometimes declare technically compliant bids "non-responsive" without adequate justification — departing from the evaluation criteria published in the tender document, applying undisclosed evaluation factors, or making errors in the bid evaluation process. Contractors whose bids have been wrongfully disqualified have the right to challenge the disqualification through the PPA Review Panel process — and, where the Review Panel fails to provide adequate relief, through a writ petition to the High Court Division. Time is critical in tender challenges: the PPA Review Panel mechanism must be invoked within strict timelines after the relevant procurement decision, and delays can result in the loss of the challenge right entirely.

2. Payment Disputes and Delayed Certificates

Payment delays — including failure to issue interim payment certificates, disputes over the valuation of work done, non-payment of retention monies after defects liability expiry, and refusal to process final account settlements — are among the most common and practically damaging problems faced by government contractors in Bangladesh. Government ministries and departments frequently face budget release delays, bureaucratic processing obstacles, and disputes over measurement and valuation that create significant cash flow stress for contractors. Where undisputed payment amounts are being withheld without lawful basis, contractors have both civil suit remedies and — where the withholding constitutes an illegal failure to perform a statutory or contractual duty — writ mandamus remedies available to compel payment.

3. Variation Order and Scope Disputes

Government infrastructure and construction contracts frequently involve scope changes, site condition variations, design modifications, and employer-instructed additional works — which give rise to variation order claims. Disputes frequently arise over: whether a particular change qualifies as a variation under the contract (and therefore attractive additional payment) or falls within the original scope; the correct valuation methodology for agreed variations; and the formal approval authority and threshold requirements under the PPR for variation orders above prescribed percentages of the original contract value. Poorly documented variation order claims — or claims submitted without strict compliance with the notice and claim procedure in the contract conditions — are systematically rejected by government procuring entities, often leaving contractors to absorb additional costs that they are contractually entitled to recover.

4. Unlawful Blacklisting

Blacklisting of contractors under Rule 127 of the PPR, 2008 — which bars a blacklisted contractor from participating in any government procurement for the duration of the blacklisting period — is one of the most commercially damaging actions a government body can take against a contractor. Rule 127 requires that before a blacklisting decision is made, the procuring entity must issue a show-cause notice to the contractor, afford the contractor a reasonable opportunity to respond, and consider the contractor's response before making a final decision. Blacklisting decisions made without following this procedural requirement — or on grounds not supported by the evidence — are unlawful and can be challenged by writ petition. The High Court Division has on numerous occasions quashed blacklisting decisions that failed to observe the procedural requirements of Rule 127.

5. Contract Termination Disputes

Government procuring entities sometimes terminate contracts — citing contractor default, delay, or performance failures — in circumstances where the termination is legally unjustified or procedurally defective. Wrongful termination of a government contract causes severe commercial damage: the contractor loses its revenue stream, its performance security is at risk of encashment, and its reputation in the public procurement market is harmed. Challenging a wrongful government contract termination — through the contract's dispute resolution process, through civil proceedings for wrongful termination damages, or through writ petition where the termination decision breaches constitutional standards of procedural fairness — requires urgent specialist legal action from the moment termination is threatened or executed.

Practical Tips for Clients

  • Act within PPA Review Panel timelines without delay: The PPA, 2006 prescribes strict timelines for filing Review Panel complaints — typically a matter of days from the relevant procurement decision. Missing these deadlines extinguishes the right to challenge through the administrative review process, leaving the more complex and time-consuming route of writ petition as the only remaining option. If you believe a procurement decision is unlawful, contact specialist legal counsel on the same day you receive notice of the decision.
  • Issue all contractual notices strictly in accordance with the contract: Most government contracts — particularly those based on CPTU Standard Bidding Documents or MDB Harmonised Conditions — require contractors to give written notice of claims within specified time periods after the event giving rise to the claim. Failure to give notice within the required time is frequently used by government procuring entities as a complete defence to an otherwise valid claim. Implement robust contract administration systems that ensure all required notices are issued on time, in the correct form, and to the designated responsible officer.
  • Document everything — contemporaneously: Government contract disputes — particularly variation claims, delay damages, and force majeure claims — are invariably won or lost on the quality of the contemporaneous documentary record. Daily site diaries, correspondence files, minutes of site meetings, photographic evidence, measurement records, and official communications must be maintained systematically throughout the contract. Attempting to reconstruct this record after a dispute arises is significantly less persuasive to courts and arbitrators than contemporaneous documentation maintained as a matter of course.
  • Respond promptly to show-cause notices: If you receive a show-cause notice from a government procuring entity under Rule 127 of the PPR — warning of potential blacklisting — treat it as a matter of immediate legal urgency. Engage specialist legal counsel to prepare your response. A well-prepared, legally accurate, and factually compelling show-cause response can prevent blacklisting — or create the record necessary for a successful High Court challenge if the procuring entity proceeds despite your response.
  • Do not rely solely on informal negotiations with government officials: While constructive dialogue with government officials is often a sensible first step in resolving a contract dispute, experienced government contractors in Bangladesh know that informal negotiations must be supported by a parallel legal track — preserving your formal rights through proper contractual notices, Review Panel filings where applicable, and legal proceedings preparation. Procuring entities are constrained by their own internal approval processes and cannot informally settle legal obligations without going through formal channels; relying solely on informal assurances of settlement frequently results in contractors losing both the dispute and the limitation period for their legal claims.
Practical Insight

Bangladesh's courts — and particularly the High Court Division's writ jurisdiction — have proved to be a genuinely effective forum for challenging unlawful government procurement decisions and compelling procuring entities to comply with their legal obligations. The High Court has quashed wrongful bid disqualifications, struck down procedurally defective blacklisting decisions, and issued mandamus orders compelling payment of undisputed contract amounts. The constitutional writ jurisdiction exists precisely because government bodies — like any public authority — must act within the law when exercising their powers. Government contractors who understand this principle and who engage specialist legal counsel to enforce their rights through the courts achieve significantly better outcomes than those who accept unlawful government action as an unavoidable feature of public sector contracting.

FAQs

1. What is the PPA Review Panel and when should I use it?
The Review Panel is a mandatory administrative dispute resolution mechanism established under the Public Procurement Act, 2006 that provides tenderers with the right to formally challenge procurement decisions — including non-responsive determinations, bid disqualifications, and evaluation irregularities — before a final contract award is made. The Review Panel process is designed to provide a quick, low-cost mechanism for resolving procurement disputes at the administrative level, without requiring recourse to the courts. To use the Review Panel, a tenderer must file a written complaint with the designated Review Panel within the strict timelines prescribed by the PPA and PPR — typically within a matter of days of receiving notice of the adverse procurement decision. The complaint must identify the specific PPA/PPR provisions you allege have been violated and provide supporting documentation. Importantly, many courts have indicated that parties should exhaust the Review Panel process before seeking writ relief in the High Court Division — making the Review Panel not merely an option but effectively a required first step in most procurement challenges. Specialist legal counsel should be instructed immediately after any adverse procurement decision to ensure that the Review Panel complaint is filed correctly and within time.

2. Can I challenge a government procurement decision in the High Court?
Yes — the High Court Division of the Supreme Court of Bangladesh has wide-ranging constitutional jurisdiction under Article 102 to review and quash unlawful government procurement decisions. Where a government procuring entity has acted unlawfully — by violating the PPA/PPR framework, applying undisclosed evaluation criteria, making a decision tainted by procedural unfairness, or refusing to comply with a legal duty — the High Court Division can issue writ orders including certiorari (to quash the unlawful decision), mandamus (to compel the procuring entity to act lawfully), and injunctions (to restrain implementation of an unlawful decision pending the court's determination). In cases involving significant commercial stakes — such as a major contract award to a competing tenderer, an imminent blacklisting, or a wrongful contract termination — obtaining an interlocutory injunction from the High Court Division can be the most effective immediate remedy available to protect your commercial position while the legal challenge proceeds. Specialist legal counsel experienced in government procurement writ proceedings is essential to prepare and present these applications effectively.

3. My government client has not paid me for months. What are my legal options?
Non-payment by government procuring entities is one of the most common complaints of contractors and suppliers engaged in public sector work in Bangladesh. Your legal options depend on the nature of the payment dispute: if the amount is undisputed and the procuring entity simply has not processed the payment certificate or released the funds, you may have both a civil claim under the contract and — where the failure to pay constitutes a failure to perform a legal duty — a writ mandamus application to the High Court Division to compel payment. If the amount is disputed — for example, if the procuring entity challenges the valuation of work done or deducts liquidated damages that you contest — the applicable dispute resolution procedure in the contract conditions (typically initial reference to the Project Director, followed by adjudication, arbitration, or court litigation depending on the contract) must be followed. In either scenario, all contractual notices and claims must be submitted in strict compliance with the contract's procedural requirements to preserve your full legal entitlement. Specialist legal counsel can assess the most effective and fastest route to payment recovery given your specific contract terms, the amount at issue, and the nature of the procuring entity involved.

4. What are my rights if I am blacklisted by a government ministry?
If a government procuring entity is considering blacklisting your business under Rule 127 of the Public Procurement Rules, 2008, you have important procedural rights that must be respected: the procuring entity must first issue you a written show-cause notice identifying the grounds for the proposed blacklisting and giving you a reasonable period to submit a written response; it must genuinely consider your response before making a final decision; and the blacklisting decision must be proportionate and based on sufficient evidence of the alleged conduct. If any of these procedural requirements are not followed — or if the blacklisting is based on factually incorrect allegations — the decision is legally vulnerable to challenge and quashing by the High Court Division. In addition to procedural challenges, there are substantive grounds on which blacklisting decisions can be attacked — including disproportionality, insufficient evidence, and failure to give adequate weight to mitigating circumstances. The most important practical point is to engage specialist legal counsel immediately upon receiving a show-cause notice — both to prepare the most compelling possible show-cause response (which may prevent the blacklisting entirely) and to preserve all legal grounds for a High Court challenge if the procuring entity proceeds despite your response.

5. How are variation order disputes resolved under government contracts?
Variation order disputes — involving disagreements about whether a change qualifies as a variation, or about the correct valuation of an agreed variation — are resolved through the dispute resolution process specified in the government contract conditions. For contracts using CPTU Standard Bidding Documents, the initial dispute resolution step is typically a reference to the Project Director or Engineer — who has the authority to make determinations on variation entitlements and values under the contract. If the contractor is dissatisfied with the Project Director's determination, the contract will typically provide for escalation to a higher-level dispute resolution mechanism — which under modern CPTU-based contracts may include adjudication, arbitration under Bangladesh's Arbitration Act, 2001, or litigation before the competent civil court. Variation claims must comply strictly with the contract's notice requirements — in many standard government contracts, failure to give notice of a potential variation within the prescribed time after the event giving rise to the claim constitutes a bar to recovery, regardless of the merit of the underlying claim. Specialist legal and contract advisory support — both in identifying and documenting variation entitlements and in pursuing them through the contract's dispute resolution process — significantly improves the commercial outcome of variation claims against government bodies.
